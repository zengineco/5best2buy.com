Table Olives Worth the Hunt

A great olive is slow food: fruit picked at the right moment and cured in brine for weeks or months until the bitterness turns to something deep and savory. The cheap can of black "ripe" olives — lye-cured fast and dyed uniform — is a different product entirely. Real table olives are meaty, bright, and buttery, from tree-ripened California heirlooms to Sicily's famous green Castelvetrano. On a pizza, in a salad, or straight from the jar, they're worth seeking out.

Straight Answers
Table Olives FAQ
What are the main ways table olives are cured?

Raw olives are inedibly bitter, so all table olives are cured. Brine-curing soaks them in salt water for weeks or months and gives the deepest, most complex flavor; dry-salt curing packs them in salt for a wrinkled, intense result; and water-curing is the gentlest. The cheap canned "black ripe" olive is lye-cured fast and then dyed uniform black, which is why it tastes flat next to a slow brine-cured olive.

Why is Castelvetrano so mild and buttery?

Castelvetrano is a green Sicilian variety picked young and cured quickly and gently, usually in a light brine, so it keeps its bright color and low-bitterness, almost buttery taste. That mildness makes it the classic "gateway olive" that converts people who think they dislike olives. Its flavor is much closer to fresh than the sharp, salty punch of a Kalamata or oil-cured black olive.

Should I buy pitted or whole olives?

Whole, un-pitted olives hold their texture and flavor better, because pitting cuts the flesh and lets brine and air work into the olive, softening it. If you are eating them straight, on a board, or on a pizza where a little effort is fine, buy them whole. Pitted olives are a convenience for cooking, tapenade, or salads where you do not want to deal with the stones.

How do you store olives once the jar is open?

Refrigerate opened olives and keep them fully submerged in their brine, which preserves both flavor and safety; the brine is not waste, it is the storage medium. If you run low on liquid, top the jar with a simple salt-water brine or a splash of water so the olives stay covered. Kept this way, table olives last for weeks, and their taste holds far better than olives left dry or exposed to air.
