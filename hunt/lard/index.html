Straight Answers
Leaf Lard & Manteca FAQ
What is leaf lard and why is it the best cooking fat?

Leaf lard is the soft fat from around the pig's kidneys — the highest, cleanest grade of lard. Mild and neutral with a high smoke point, it makes the flakiest pastry and the most authentic refried beans and carnitas. Other lards can taste porky; leaf lard doesn't.

What is the difference between lard, manteca, and shortening?

Manteca is simply Spanish for lard. Real lard is rendered pork fat. Shortening is industrially hydrogenated vegetable oil, historically with trans fats — a modern substitute that lard predates by centuries. In the artisan market, manteca is sold as leaf lard, so that's what to hunt for.

Is lard actually healthy?

Real, non-hydrogenated leaf lard has no trans fat and is largely monounsaturated — closer to olive oil than to shortening. The old health scare was about hydrogenated commercial lard, not the pastured, rendered kind. As a whole natural fat, it's a reasonable choice used in moderation.

How do I store lard?

Keep it in the fridge, where it lasts many months, or freeze it for a year. Good pasture-raised lard is soft and white and smells clean and neutral, not porky. If it smells off or rancid, discard it.
