Cashews Worth the Hunt

Almost no cashews are grown in the US - they're a tropical nut - and the supply chain behind the cheap bag is grim: the shell is caustic, and most cashews are hand-cracked by underpaid, often unprotected workers in Asia. The makers here buy fair-trade or direct, so the people who shelled your cashews got paid and protected. A few roast them into something worth the jar.

Straight Answers
Cashews FAQ
Why are cashews never sold in the shell?

The shell contains urushiol, the same caustic oil as poison ivy, so cashews must be roasted or steamed and hand-shelled before sale. That shelling is skilled, hazardous work, often done by hand in South and Southeast Asia. It's the human cost hidden in a cheap bag, and the reason fair-trade sourcing matters more for cashews than almost any other nut.

Are cashews grown in the United States?

Essentially no - the cashew tree needs a tropical climate and the US has no commercial crop. So a 'local' cashew doesn't exist; the meaningful choice is how they were sourced and processed. Fair-trade and direct-trade brands pay farmers and shellers fairly, which is the independent story here.

What's the difference between raw and roasted cashews?

'Raw' cashews aren't truly raw - they've already been heated to remove the toxic shell, just not roasted for flavor. Roasted cashews are cooked again to deepen the taste and crunch. Dry-roasted (like Tierra Farm's) uses no oil; oil-roasted adds fat and usually more salt.

How should I store cashews so they don't go rancid?

Cashews are high in oil and turn rancid faster than you'd think at room temperature. Keep them sealed in the fridge for a few months, or the freezer for up to a year - they thaw in minutes. Buy from a maker with real turnover so they're fresh when they reach you.
