Straight Answers
Plant-Only FAQ
What is the difference between vegan and plant-based on a label?

Vegan means no animal-derived ingredients at all, and it is the stricter, more specific claim. Plant-based is a looser marketing term that usually signals a mostly-plant product but is not a guarantee, and some plant-based foods still contain small amounts of dairy, egg, or honey. For a truly animal-free product, look for a clear vegan claim or certification rather than the softer phrase.

What hidden animal ingredients should I watch for?

Plenty of unobvious ingredients come from animals: gelatin, honey, casein and whey (from milk), carmine or cochineal (a red color from insects), some vitamin D3 (often from lanolin), certain natural flavors, and confectioner's glaze (from lac insects). Cane sugar is sometimes filtered with bone char, and wine or vinegar can use animal finings. This is exactly why a short, transparent ingredient list makes plant-only easier to trust.

Can plant-only food be nutritionally complete?

Yes, a well-planned plant-only diet can meet nutritional needs, but a few nutrients need attention. Vitamin B12 is the big one, since it is not reliably present in plant foods and generally needs a supplement or fortified foods. Iron, zinc, omega-3s, calcium, and vitamin D are worth planning for too. Whole-food plant products help, but variety and, where needed, supplementation are what make it complete.

Why does vegan cheese from small makers cost more?

Cultured nut-based cheeses are slow, real work: soaking and blending nuts like cashews, adding live cultures, and aging the result over days or weeks, in small batches. That is a world away from a cheap starch-and-oil block, and the price reflects the ingredients and the time. What you get is genuine fermentation and flavor rather than a rubbery imitation.
