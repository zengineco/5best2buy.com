Frozen Dumplings & Potstickers Worth the Hunt

Most bagged supermarket dumplings are gummy wrappers around a pea-sized knot of filling, pumped out on a line and frozen weeks ago. The good ones are pleated by hand in small kitchens, flash-frozen the day they're made, and shipped to you on dry ice. Cook them straight from the freezer — steamed, pan-fried, or boiled — and they taste like a restaurant made them, because one did.

Straight Answers
Frozen Dumplings & Potstickers FAQ
Do I thaw frozen dumplings before cooking?

No — cook them straight from frozen. Thawing makes the wrappers stick together and go soggy, and soup dumplings will leak their broth. Drop them frozen into steam, boiling water, or a hot oiled pan and add a couple minutes to the cook time.

What's the difference between potstickers, soup dumplings, and pelmeni?

Potstickers (guotie) are pan-fried dumplings, crisp on the bottom and steamed on top. Soup dumplings (xiao long bao) hold seasoned broth inside a delicate skin and are steamed. Pelmeni are small Eastern European meat dumplings, usually boiled. Same idea — dough around filling — with different skins, fillings, and cooking methods.

How do I steam or pan-fry them without a mess?

For potstickers: heat oil in a pan, add frozen dumplings, then pour in a splash of water and cover so they steam, uncovering at the end to crisp the bottoms. For steamed dumplings and soup dumplings, use a lined steamer basket so they don't stick — parchment or a cabbage leaf works. Never crowd them.

Why do these ship on dry ice instead of just cold packs?

Handmade dumplings are flash-frozen and need to stay frozen the whole trip, and dry ice holds a much lower temperature than gel packs. It usually arrives still smoking and the dumplings rock-hard. Move them to your freezer right away; if any are soft, cook them within a day.
