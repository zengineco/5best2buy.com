5 Craft Drinking Chocolate Makers Worth the Hunt

Most "hot chocolate" in a store is a packet of sugar, powdered dairy, and a little cocoa. Real drinking chocolate is closer to a melted bar: actual ground cacao, thick and rich, the way it was drunk for centuries before it became a powder. The makers below are small bean-to-bar and stone-ground producers who care where their cacao comes from, and they all make a dedicated version meant to be drunk rather than eaten. Here's where to buy it.

Published June 2026 · Updated 29 Jun 2026

How this list works. Every maker here controls its own cacao from bean to cup, makes a real drinking or sipping chocolate, and earns its spot on merit — nobody pays to be listed. In chocolate, ethics means sourcing, so that's weighted heavily: direct trade, named origins, transparency. These are deliberately different makers from our bean-to-bar chocolate shelf — no double-stocking.
No.1 · The Award-Winner

Ritual Chocolate

Heber City, UT · single-origin bean-to-bar

Ritual is a well-regarded Utah bean-to-bar maker whose ground drinking chocolate is a favorite among people who take the cup seriously. It's made from the same carefully-sourced beans as their bars, ground for melting into milk or water, and the result tastes like chocolate rather than sweetened cocoa. If you've only ever had drinking chocolate from a packet, this is the one that reframes what the drink can be.

Why it isn't on AmazonSingle-origin drinking chocolate is made in small runs from specific beans. The sourcing and the small scale are the point, and neither survives a commodity listing.

No.2 · The Two-Ingredient One

Xocolatl Small Batch

Atlanta, GA · single-origin, minimal-ingredient

Xocolatl is a small Atlanta maker working single-origin Nicaraguan cacao with real transparency about where it comes from. Their powdered drinking chocolate keeps the ingredient list short — cacao and little else — so what you taste is the bean, not filler. They also make a cacao tea from the husks, which is an unusual, lightly chocolatey thing worth trying if you like the idea of using the whole pod.

Why it isn't on AmazonA two-ingredient, single-origin product from a tiny maker is defined by its sourcing. That's a direct relationship with a farm, not a commodity stream.

See it at Xocolatl →
No.3 · The Conservation One

Castronovo Chocolate

Stuart, FL · founder-ecologist, direct sourcing

Castronovo is a small, award-heavy maker founded by an ecologist, with cacao sourcing tied to rainforest protection and direct relationships with growers in Colombia and beyond. The ethics here aren't a marketing layer; they're the reason the company exists. Their stone-ground and single-origin offerings make a deep, genuinely good cup, and buying it supports sourcing that treats the forest and the farmers as part of the product.

Why it isn't on AmazonConservation-linked, direct-sourced cacao is produced in small, traceable lots. The whole model depends on relationships a commodity platform can't represent.

See it at Castronovo →
No.4 · The Single-Origin Specialist

Fruition Chocolate Works

Shokan, NY · single-origin bean-to-bar

Fruition is a Hudson Valley bean-to-bar maker with a serious reputation for single-origin work, and their drinking chocolate carries the same attention to where the beans come from. It melts into a smooth, full cup with real depth, and because they roast and grind everything in-house, the flavor is theirs rather than a generic chocolate base. A good pick if you want a maker who treats the drink as seriously as the bar.

Why it isn't on AmazonIn-house single-origin production runs in small batches tied to specific harvests. That traceability and scale don't translate to a commodity case.

See it at Fruition →
No.5 · The Mayan-Style Cup

SOMA Chocolate

Toronto, ON · bean-to-bar, Mayan-style drinking chocolate

SOMA is a celebrated Toronto bean-to-bar maker whose Mayan-style drinking chocolate is closer to a spiced, intense ceremonial cup than anything in a grocery aisle. It's thick, complex, and made from beans they roast and grind themselves, which is why it tastes nothing like a powder. For anyone who wants drinking chocolate at its richest and most traditional, this is the one to seek out.

Why it isn't on AmazonA small maker's traditional-style drinking chocolate is a craft product made in limited quantity. It sells from their own shop and counter, not a commodity shelf.
