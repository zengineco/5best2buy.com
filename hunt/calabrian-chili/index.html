Calabrian Chili Worth the Hunt

Calabrian chilies are the secret weapon of great pizza and pasta — a bright, fruity, moderate heat with none of the flat burn of dried flakes. Grown in the far south of Italy and packed whole, crushed, or as a paste (the famous "bomba") in olive oil, they melt into a sauce, dot a pizza, or spike a vinaigrette. A spoonful transforms a dish. Once a jar lives in your fridge, you'll reach for it constantly.

Straight Answers
Calabrian Chili FAQ
What's the difference between whole, paste, and oil-packed Calabrian chilies?

Whole or sliced chilies packed in oil give you the most control — you chop or mince as much as a dish needs, and the oil itself is flavored. Crushed chilies fall between whole and paste, ready to spoon but still textured. The paste, often sold as "bomba," is the peppers blended smooth with oil, vinegar, and salt, so it spreads and melts into a sauce fastest. For everyday cooking, the crushed version is the most versatile workhorse.

How hot is Calabrian chili?

It sits in a pleasant medium range — noticeably hotter than a jalapeño but well short of a habanero, roughly comparable to a serrano. What sets it apart is that the heat arrives with a fruity, almost smoky sweetness rather than a flat burn, which is why it flatters food instead of overwhelming it. A single spoonful adds warmth and depth without turning a dish into a heat challenge.

How do you cook with Calabrian chili?

Stir a spoonful of the paste or crushed chilies into a pan sauce, marinara, or a pot of beans; smear it on pizza dough before the cheese; whisk it into a vinaigrette or aioli; or toss it with roasted vegetables and fish. The oil the chilies are packed in is a bonus — use it to finish a dish. Because the flavor is bright, add it toward the end so it stays vivid.

How should you store an open jar of Calabrian chili?

Refrigerate after opening and keep the chilies submerged under their oil, which acts as a natural seal against air. Use a clean, dry spoon each time to avoid introducing moisture, and top with a little olive oil if the surface is exposed. Stored this way, an open jar keeps well for months, and the flavored oil is worth using rather than discarding.
