Straight Answers
Olive Oil FAQ
What does "extra virgin" actually mean?

Extra virgin is the top grade: oil pressed from olives by mechanical means only, with no heat or chemical solvents, and a free-acidity level under 0.8 percent with no sensory defects. The problem is that the grade is widely abused, so the label alone guarantees little. A harvest date and a named single estate tell you far more than the words "extra virgin" ever will.

Why does the harvest date matter more than a best-by date?

Olive oil is a fresh-pressed fruit juice, not a preserved product, and it starts fading the moment it is milled. A harvest or crush date tells you how young the oil actually is; a printed best-by date can sit two years out and hide oil that is already tired. Aim to use good oil within a year of harvest, and buy from producers who print the date they pressed it.

How should I store olive oil so it lasts?

Heat, light, and air are what turn oil rancid, so keep it in a cool, dark cupboard away from the stove, tightly capped, and in dark glass or tin rather than clear bottles. Do not refrigerate it — that is unnecessary and only clouds it. Buy a size you will finish in a couple of months rather than a giant tin that goes stale before you reach the bottom.

Can I cook and fry with good olive oil, or only finish with it?

You can absolutely cook with it. Extra virgin olive oil has a smoke point around 375 to 405 degrees Fahrenheit, which comfortably covers sautéing, roasting, and most pan frying. Its antioxidants actually make it more stable than many people assume. Save your most peppery, expensive single-estate bottle for finishing raw, where you can taste every note, and use an everyday bottle for the heat.
