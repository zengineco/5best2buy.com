5 Heirloom Bean Growers Worth the Hunt

A dried bean from the grocery store is anonymous, often years old, and grown for yield. Heirloom beans are the opposite: named varieties with their own colors, textures, and flavors, grown by small farms that saved the seed because the bean was worth saving. They cook up creamier and taste like something, and several on this list are grown by the Indigenous communities that have stewarded them for generations. Here's where to buy them.

Straight Answers
Heirloom Bean FAQ
Why does bean freshness matter if they are dried anyway?

Dried does not mean immortal. A bean that has sat for years dehydrates further and its skin toughens, so it cooks unevenly and may never soften no matter how long you simmer. Heirloom beans from a small grower are usually from a recent harvest, which is why they cook faster and more evenly and hold their shape. A known crop year, which good growers list, is the single best predictor of a bean that cooks well.

Do you really need to soak heirloom beans?

Soaking is optional but helpful. An overnight soak shortens cooking time and helps beans cook more evenly, and it is worth doing for older or larger varieties. Because heirloom beans are typically fresh, many cooks skip the soak and simmer them straight, which some argue gives a creamier pot and more flavorful broth. Either way, salt the cooking water; the old warning that salt toughens beans is largely a myth with fresh ones.

What makes heirloom beans taste better than a grocery bag?

Commodity beans are anonymous, bred for yield and uniformity, and often old by the time you buy them. Heirloom varieties like tepary, Jacob's Cattle, and scarlet runner were saved because they taste like something, each with its own texture, color, and character, and they are grown fresh on small farms. Tepary beans in particular are dense and nutty, unlike anything in a supermarket aisle. The pot liquor alone tells you the difference.

How should you store dried heirloom beans?

Keep them in an airtight container in a cool, dark, dry place, away from heat and light, which speed the staling that toughens the skin. Stored well they are good for a year or two, but they cook best within the first year, so buy amounts you will actually use rather than hoarding. Note the harvest year when you buy, and cook your oldest beans first so none drift past their prime.
