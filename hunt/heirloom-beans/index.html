5 Heirloom Bean Growers Worth the Hunt

A dried bean from the grocery store is anonymous, often years old, and grown for yield. Heirloom beans are the opposite: named varieties with their own colors, textures, and flavors, grown by small farms that saved the seed because the bean was worth saving. They cook up creamier and taste like something, and several on this list are grown by the Indigenous communities that have stewarded them for generations. Here's where to buy them.

No.1 · The Indigenous Family Farm

Ramona Farms

Sacaton, AZ · Akimel O'odham family farm

Ramona Farms is an Akimel O'odham family farm on the Gila River Indian Community, growing brown, white, and black tepary beans and heritage garbanzos on tribal land — varieties their community has cultivated for centuries. Tepary beans are drought-adapted, dense, and nutty, unlike anything in a grocery aisle, and buying them supports an Indigenous farm keeping its own foodways alive. This is provenance in its truest form. Start with the teparies.

Why it isn't on AmazonThese are culturally-specific varieties grown by one family on tribal land in limited quantity. The story and the stewardship are inseparable from the bean, and a commodity listing can't hold either.

No.2 · The Seed Conservancy

Native Seeds/SEARCH

Tucson, AZ · nonprofit, arid-adapted heirlooms

Native Seeds/SEARCH is a Tucson nonprofit that conserves arid-adapted heirloom crops of the Southwest, working directly with Indigenous growers to keep tepary beans and Four Corners varieties in cultivation. Buying from them funds seed conservation — you're not just getting beans, you're keeping rare varieties from disappearing. It's the most mission-aligned name in the whole category, and the beans are genuinely special.

Why it isn't on AmazonA conservation nonprofit grows tiny quantities of rare, regionally-specific seed. The mission is the model, and it has nothing to do with a commodity supply chain.

See it at Native Seeds/SEARCH →
No.3 · The Bean Workhorse

Zursun Idaho Heirloom Beans

Twin Falls, ID · 40+ varieties since 1985

Zursun has sourced heirloom beans from small family farms in Idaho's Snake River Canyon since 1985, with a roster of more than forty varieties — a peer to the better-known California names, with an even deeper catalog. If you want to actually explore heirloom beans rather than buy one bag, this is the grower with the range to do it. Their scarlet runners and flageolets are good places to wander in.

Why it isn't on AmazonA curated roster of small-farm heirloom varieties depends on relationships with specific growers and small harvests. That selection is the value a commodity listing can't replicate.

No.4 · The One Maine Farm

Baer's Best Beans

South Berwick, ME · single-farm New England heirlooms

Baer's Best is one Maine farm growing New England heirloom beans — Jacob's Cattle, Marfax, Yellow Eye — some of which were originally cultivated by the region's Indigenous peoples. It's about as single-farm as it gets, with a small minimum order and beans you'll struggle to find anywhere else. These are the regional varieties that built New England cooking, kept going by one grower who cares about them.

Why it isn't on AmazonA single farm growing regional heirlooms produces a finite harvest sold direct. There's no commodity stream behind it — you buy it from the farm or you don't get it.

See it at Baer's Best →
No.5 · The Organic California Farm

Blue House Farm

San Gregorio, CA · CCOF-organic, 30+ varieties

Blue House Farm is a CCOF-certified-organic farm on the San Mateo coast growing more than thirty heirloom bean varieties alongside its vegetables. These are beans grown on a real, diversified organic farm rather than sourced and repackaged, dried down from the same fields that supply Bay Area markets. A great pick if you want heirloom beans with a clear single-farm, certified-organic backstory.

Why it isn't on AmazonA diversified organic farm grows beans in small rotations as part of a larger system. That farm-direct, certified sourcing is the value, and it doesn't scale into a commodity shelf.
