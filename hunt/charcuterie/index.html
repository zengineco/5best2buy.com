Straight Answers
Charcuterie FAQ
What's the difference between salumi, salami, and charcuterie?

Charcuterie is the broad French term for prepared and cured meats of all kinds — pâtés, terrines, cured whole muscles, and dry sausages. Salumi is the Italian equivalent, covering everything from prosciutto to coppa. Salami is one specific category within that: a fermented, dry-cured sausage of ground meat and fat stuffed into a casing. So all salami is salumi, but salumi and charcuterie include much more than salami.

How do I build a good charcuterie board?

Aim for variety in texture and intensity rather than quantity: a firm dry salami, a softer spreadable one, and a whole-muscle cure like coppa or prosciutto covers a lot of ground. Plan on roughly two to three ounces of cured meat per person as an appetizer, and let everything come to room temperature before serving so the fat softens and the aromas open up. Slice dry salami thin and on a slight angle, and pair with something acidic — pickles, mustard, or a sharp cheese — to cut the richness.

Do I need to worry about the white mold on the outside of salami?

No — the powdery white coating on a good dry salami is an intentional, edible mold culture, usually a Penicillium strain, applied to protect the meat and develop flavor as it cures. It's a sign of traditional production, not spoilage. You can eat it or wipe/peel it off if you prefer; fuzzy green, black, or slimy patches are a different story and mean the piece has gone off.

How should I store cured salami once I've bought it?

An unopened, fully dry-cured salami is shelf-stable and can hang in a cool, dry spot, but once cut it's best kept in the fridge wrapped in parchment or butcher paper rather than airtight plastic, which traps moisture and can turn the surface slimy. Whole-muscle and softer cures should stay refrigerated. If a hard salami dries out further, that's normal — it just gets firmer and more concentrated, not unsafe.
