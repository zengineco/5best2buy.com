Straight Answers
Heirloom Seeds FAQ
What is the difference between heirloom, open-pollinated, and hybrid seed?

Open-pollinated means the plant pollinates naturally and its seed grows back true to type, so you can save and replant it. Heirloom is an open-pollinated variety with a history — usually passed down for 50 years or more. Hybrid (often labeled F1) is a deliberate cross bred for uniformity or shipping; its saved seed will not grow true, so you have to rebuy it each year. Every company on this shelf sells open-pollinated, saveable seed.

Can I really save seed from these and regrow them?

Yes — that is the whole point of open-pollinated varieties. Let the fruit fully ripen, harvest and dry the seed, and store it cool and dark. A few crops (squash, corn, cucumbers) cross-pollinate readily, so you may need to isolate or hand-pollinate them to keep a variety pure, but tomatoes, beans, peppers, and lettuce are easy for a beginner to save reliably.

Does "non-GMO" and the Safe Seed Pledge actually mean anything?

Genetically engineered seed is not sold to home gardeners in the first place, so "non-GMO" on a seed packet is more a statement of values than a practical safeguard. The Safe Seed Pledge is a public commitment a company signs to not knowingly sell genetically engineered seed — a signal that the company sits firmly in the independent, open-pollinated tradition. It is a marker of who you are buying from as much as what.

How long does seed stay viable, and how should I store it?

Most vegetable seed stays viable for several years if kept cool, dark, and dry — a sealed jar in the fridge is ideal. Some (onions, leeks, parsnips) fade fast and are best used within a year; others (tomatoes, beans, brassicas) can last four to six. If you are unsure, run a quick germination test on a damp paper towel before committing a whole bed to old seed.
