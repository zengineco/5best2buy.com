Straight Answers
Sourdough Starter FAQ
What is the difference between a live starter and a dehydrated one?

A live starter arrives active and wet, already fed and bubbling, so you can refresh it once and bake within a day or two. A dehydrated starter is that same culture dried into flakes for shelf stability; it ships lighter and keeps longer, but you have to rehydrate and coax it back to strength over several feedings before it will raise a loaf. The makers here ship live cultures, which skip the revival and the uncertainty.

How do you activate and feed a starter when it arrives?

Move it to a clean jar and feed it with equal parts flour and water by weight, then leave it at warm room temperature. Within hours a healthy live culture will bubble and roughly double, which tells you it is ready to bake. From there, feed it on a regular schedule if you bake often, or store it fed in the fridge and refresh it before use. Every seller here includes their own feeding guidance.

Does a starter's age or lineage actually change the bread?

An older, well-kept culture tends to be more vigorous and resilient because its yeast and bacteria have balanced over time, which shows up as a more reliable rise and deeper flavor. Regional lineage matters too; a documented San Francisco culture carries the tang that made the city famous. That said, your flour, water, and kitchen also shape the result, so a starter drifts toward your own environment over time.

Why buy a starter instead of making your own from scratch?

You can absolutely raise a starter from just flour and water, but it takes a week or two of daily feeding and can stall or sour wrong along the way. Buying a mature culture gives you months of someone else's patient work on arrival, active and ready, which is the whole product. It is also the only way to get a specific heritage or century-old lineage that no home starter can replicate.
