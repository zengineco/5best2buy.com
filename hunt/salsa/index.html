Straight Answers
Salsa FAQ
What is salsa macha, and how is it different from regular salsa?

Salsa macha comes from Veracruz and is essentially Mexico's answer to chili crisp: dried chilies, garlic, and often nuts or seeds fried in oil, so it is rich, crunchy, and oil-based rather than the wet, tomato-forward salsa most Americans know. It keeps far longer than fresh salsa and gets spooned over eggs, tacos, grilled meat, or roasted vegetables. Its depth comes from toasting rather than from tomatoes.

Why does jarred grocery salsa taste so different from fresh?

Shelf-stable jarred salsa is cooked and heavily acidified with vinegar so it survives on a warm shelf for a year, which dulls the fresh chile and tomato flavor and adds a sour, processed edge. Small makers use real chiles, shorter cooks, and fewer preservatives, so the result tastes closer to something made in a kitchen. The trade-off is a shorter fridge life once opened.

What makes Hatch chile salsa special?

Hatch chiles are grown in the Hatch Valley of southern New Mexico, where the soil and climate give them an earthy, bright, slightly smoky flavor that growers argue cannot be replicated elsewhere. They are usually fire-roasted, which deepens that flavor further. The season is short — late summer — so much of the year's crop is roasted and preserved, which is why fire-roasted Hatch salsa from the source is prized.

How should I store salsa, and how long does it keep?

Fresh or refrigerated salsa should stay cold and is usually best within about a week to ten days of opening. Oil-based salsa macha is more forgiving — the oil protects it, so a jar keeps for weeks, though refrigeration extends it and firms the oil, which liquefies again at room temperature. Always use a clean spoon, and discard any salsa that smells fermented or grows mold.
