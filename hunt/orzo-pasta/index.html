Straight Answers
Orzo & Mediterranean Pasta FAQ
Is orzo pasta or rice?

It's pasta — made from durum wheat semolina, just shaped like a large grain of rice (in Italian it's often called risoni, 'big rice'). It cooks like pasta, in boiling salted water in under ten minutes, and contains gluten. It's not a rice substitute for anyone avoiding wheat, but it's a fast, versatile swap for rice in salads and pilafs.

Why does bronze-die pasta cost more, and is it worth it?

Bronze dies scrape the dough as it's extruded, leaving a rough, chalky surface that grips sauce far better than the slick finish from a Teflon die. It also wears the dies down faster and dries more slowly, which costs the maker more. For a dish where sauce clinging matters, it's a real, tastable upgrade; for a brothy soup, it matters less.

How do I keep orzo from turning into a sticky clump?

Cook it in plenty of well-salted boiling water, stir in the first minute so it doesn't stick, and don't overcook it — pull it at al dente. If you're using it in a cold salad, toss it with a little oil or the dressing right after draining. For pilaf style, toast it in oil first and cook it in broth like rice.

What else besides orzo counts as 'Mediterranean pasta'?

Small shapes like ditalini and stelline for soups, plus larger regional shapes — but orzo is the workhorse of Greek and eastern-Mediterranean cooking specifically. It's the base for avgolemono soup, Greek baked orzo (giouvetsi), and countless cold salads with feta, olives, and herbs. If you keep one Mediterranean pasta in the pantry, make it orzo.
