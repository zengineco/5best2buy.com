5 Craft Flour Mills Worth the Hunt

Commodity flour is degermed, aged, and often bleached — stripped of the germ so it can sit on a shelf for a year without going stale. What's stripped is most of the flavor and nearly all the nutrition. Freshly stone-milled flour keeps the whole grain intact, which is why it tastes like something and why it doesn't keep long. The mills below grind organic, heritage, and ancient grains to order and ship them fresh. For bread, pizza, or a potica dough that has to stretch paper-thin, the flour is the foundation — start here.

Straight Answers
Baking Flour FAQ
What is the difference between bread flour, all-purpose, and pastry flour?

The practical difference is protein, which governs how much gluten forms. Bread flour runs high, roughly 12 to 14 percent, for the strong, stretchy structure a risen loaf needs. All-purpose sits in the middle, around 10 to 12 percent, as a compromise for everyday baking. Pastry flour is low, near 8 to 9 percent, for tender cakes, biscuits, and pie crust that should not turn tough. Match the flour to the job and the recipe behaves.

Why does freshly stone-milled flour taste better but keep less well?

Stone milling grinds the whole grain, germ included, so the flour keeps its oils, flavor, and nutrition. Commodity flour is roller-milled and degermed precisely to remove those oils, because they are what goes rancid, letting the flour sit on a shelf for a year. Fresh flour trades shelf life for taste. Buy it in amounts you will use within a few weeks and store it cool, or freeze it to slow the oxidation.

Is 00 pizza flour the same as bread flour?

No, and it is worth keeping them separate. The 00 refers to how finely the flour is milled, a talc-fine grind traditional for Neapolitan pizza and fresh pasta, not to protein level. It gives dough extensibility and a tender, blistered crust under high heat. Bread flour is a coarser grind chosen for strength and rise. For pizza specifically, look for a real Type 00; several mills here carry one alongside their bread flours.

What are heritage and ancient grains, and why bake with them?

Heritage wheats like Turkey Red and White Sonora are older varieties grown before wheat was bred for industrial yield, and ancient grains include einkorn, farro, and emmer that predate modern wheat entirely. They carry distinctive flavor and are grown in small volumes, milled fresh, which is why you find them at small mills rather than on a grocery shelf. Some people who struggle with modern wheat report heritage varieties sit differently. They behave differently in dough, so expect to adjust hydration and rise.
