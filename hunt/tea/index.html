5 Independent Tea Companies Worth the Hunt

Grocery-store tea is mostly dust and fannings in a bag, blended from who-knows-where. Loose-leaf from a real importer is a different drink entirely — whole leaves, single regions, often traceable to the family farm that grew them. The companies below mostly buy direct, several name their growers, and all of them ship. If you've only had tea from a box, start with a style you already like and taste the gap.

Straight Answers
Tea FAQ
Is loose-leaf really better than bags, or is that snobbery?

Most grocery bags hold fannings and dust — the broken bits left after the whole leaves are sorted out, which brew fast, flat, and often bitter. Whole loose leaves unfurl in the water and give you the aroma and nuance the plant actually has, plus they can be steeped more than once. A cheap infuser basket or teapot is all the equipment you need. The gap is real, not snobbery.

What water temperature should I use?

Temperature matters more than almost anything else. Green and white teas want cooler water, roughly 160 to 180F, or they turn bitter and grassy; boiling water scorches them. Oolongs sit in the middle, and black teas and Wuyi rock teas take a full boil near 205F. If you have no thermometer, let boiled water rest a couple of minutes before pouring it on greens.

Can I re-steep the same leaves?

Yes, and with good whole-leaf tea you should. Quality oolongs and pu-erh in particular open up over several infusions, each one tasting a little different from the last, which is a large part of why loose-leaf goes further than it looks. Steep a bit longer with each round as the leaves give up less. Grocery-bag dust is spent after one cup, which is another reason whole leaf is worth it.

How long does loose-leaf tea keep?

Tea does not spoil, but it fades. Green and lightly-oxidized teas lose their fresh character within a year and are best drunk young, while heavily-oxidized blacks and roasted oolongs hold longer, and pu-erh is meant to age. Store any of it airtight, away from light, heat, moisture, and strong odors — tea readily absorbs whatever is around it. Buy amounts you will drink in a season or two rather than stockpiling.
