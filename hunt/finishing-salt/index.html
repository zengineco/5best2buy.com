5 Finishing Salt Makers Worth the Hunt

All table salt is basically the same; finishing salt is not. A flake of hand-harvested sea salt added at the end gives you crunch and a clean burst of flavor that a shaker of fine salt can't. The producers below harvest or make salt the slow way — solar evaporation, traditional pans, hand-raking — and several smoke or single-source it from a specific place. It lasts forever and changes how your food tastes, which makes it one of the cheapest real upgrades in a kitchen. Here's where to buy it.

Straight Answers
Finishing Salt FAQ
What is the difference between flaky salt, fleur de sel, and table salt?

Flaky salt (like Maldon or Jacobsen) forms thin, crunchy pyramids that shatter on the tongue — the go-to for a crisp final sprinkle. Fleur de sel is hand-skimmed from the top of salt ponds, moist and delicate, prized on subtle dishes and even desserts. Table salt is fine, dense, uniform, and usually iodized with anti-caking agents — built for baking precision and salting pasta water, not for finishing.

When should I add finishing salt?

At the very end, after the food is plated or off the heat. The whole point is texture and a clean burst of salinity you feel as distinct crunch, and that is lost if the flakes dissolve into the cooking. Season your food normally during cooking with fine salt, then finish with a pinch of flake right before it hits the table.

Is expensive sea salt actually healthier than table salt?

Not meaningfully. By weight it is nearly all sodium chloride, and the trace minerals in artisan sea salt are too small to matter nutritionally. What you are paying for is texture, a cleaner or more complex flavor, and provenance — the reasons a cook wants it. If your table salt is iodized, note that most finishing salts are not, so do not rely on them for dietary iodine.

Does finishing salt go bad, and how do I store it?

Salt does not spoil — it lasts essentially forever. The only enemy is moisture, which makes flakes clump, and strong odors, which delicate salt can pick up. Keep it in a sealed jar or a covered salt cellar away from the steam of the stove. Smoked and infused salts hold their aroma longest when kept airtight.
