Straight Answers
Hot Sauce FAQ
What makes a fermented hot sauce different?

Fermented hot sauce ferments the peppers in a salt brine for days or weeks before blending, the way sauerkraut or kimchi is made, rather than just blending raw peppers with vinegar. That process develops a deeper, funkier, more complex flavor and mellows the raw pepper's harshness. It also means less vinegar is needed, so you taste the pepper and the ferment instead of mostly acid.

Why are most grocery hot sauces so vinegar-forward?

Vinegar is cheap, preserves the sauce indefinitely at room temperature, and delivers instant tang, which is why the big mass brands lean on it. It is not wrong — a Louisiana-style vinegar sauce is a legitimate classic — but it flattens the pepper's own flavor. Craft makers use fruit, vegetables, fermentation, or a lighter vinegar hand so the sauce tastes of something beyond sour heat.

How do I tell how hot a sauce will be?

The pepper is the main clue: jalapeño and Fresno sauces are mild, habanero and Scotch bonnet are hot but fruity, and anything built on ghost pepper, scorpion, or Carolina Reaper is in serious territory. Many labels list a Scoville estimate, but a sauce with a fruit-and-vegetable base can taste far more balanced than its number suggests. When in doubt, start with a drop.

Does craft hot sauce need refrigeration?

Vinegar-based sauces are shelf-stable and fine in the pantry, but low-vinegar and fermented sauces are best kept in the fridge after opening to preserve their flavor and active cultures. Refrigeration also slows the color change from bright to dull that pepper sauces undergo over time. A darkened sauce is usually still fine to eat — it is oxidation, not spoilage — but cold storage keeps it tasting fresher longer.
