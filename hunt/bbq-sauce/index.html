Straight Answers
BBQ Sauce FAQ
What are the main regional barbecue sauce styles?

Eastern North Carolina runs on a thin vinegar-and-pepper sauce with no tomato; the western part of the state adds a little ketchup. South Carolina is famous for its tangy yellow mustard "Carolina Gold." Kansas City is the thick, sweet, tomato-and-molasses style most people picture, while Texas leans savory with more smoke, pepper, and less sugar. Alabama even does a mayonnaise-based white sauce for chicken.

Why do so many barbecue sauces use corn syrup, and does it matter?

High-fructose corn syrup is cheap and gives a uniform, aggressive sweetness, which is why it dominates the mass-market shelf. Makers who use cane sugar, molasses, or even vegetables like butternut squash and carrots get a more layered sweetness that lets the smoke, vinegar, and spice come through. It is less about health claims than balance — corn-syrup sauces tend to taste one-note and cloying.

What's the difference between a barbecue sauce, a mop, and a glaze?

A thin vinegar sauce or "mop" is brushed on during cooking to keep meat moist and add tang, and its acidity stands up to heat. A thicker, sweeter tomato-based sauce is best applied near the end or at the table, because its sugar burns if it sits over high heat too long. Think of the vinegar styles as cooking tools and the sweet styles as finishers.

Do you need to refrigerate barbecue sauce after opening?

Yes — once opened, barbecue sauce should be refrigerated, and most bottles keep well for several months in the fridge. The vinegar, salt, and sugar act as preservatives, but the tomato and low-sugar or no-corn-syrup formulas benefit especially from staying cold. If a sauce ever smells off, ferments, or grows anything on top, discard it rather than risk it.
