Straight Answers
Jerky FAQ
What makes a good jerky versus the gas-station kind?

Cheap jerky leans on sugar, sodium nitrite, MSG, and mystery-origin beef to hit a price and a long shelf life. Good jerky starts with a short, readable ingredient list and real sourcing — grass-fed beef or responsibly raised game — where the meat itself carries the flavor. Whole-muscle jerky (sliced from an actual cut) beats formed or "chopped and pressed" jerky, and a clean cure lets the meat taste like meat instead of candy.

Is exotic game jerky like elk, bison, or alligator ethical?

It can be, and it comes down to how the animal was raised or managed. Farm-raised bison and U.S.-farmed alligator, plus responsibly managed elk, venison, and wild boar, are not endangered and can be a sound choice. The thing to avoid is jerky made from genuinely endangered or poached wild species — a reputable maker will tell you plainly that their game is farmed or responsibly sourced.

What does "nitrite-free" jerky actually mean?

Conventional jerky often uses added sodium nitrite as a curing agent and preservative. "Nitrite-free" or naturally cured jerky skips the synthetic version, usually relying on salt, the drying itself, and sometimes celery-derived nitrate for preservation. It's a cleaner label, but because it has fewer preservatives it tends to have a shorter shelf life and is best kept cool and eaten sooner.

How long does jerky keep, and how should I store it?

Commercial, vacuum-sealed jerky is shelf-stable for months unopened, but once the bag is open the clock speeds up — air and moisture are its enemies. Reseal it tightly and, for clean small-batch jerky with few preservatives, keep it in the fridge and finish it within a week or two. If it looks damp, smells off, or grows any mold, throw it out; properly dried jerky should be firm and dry, not tacky-wet.
