Straight Answers
Octopus FAQ
How do I cook octopus so it isn't rubbery?

Rubbery octopus is almost always undercooked or cooked too fast. The connective tissue needs long, gentle heat to break down, so simmer or braise a whole octopus slowly for roughly 45 minutes to an hour and a half depending on size, until a knife slides easily into the thickest part of a tentacle. Only then char it hot on a grill or in a pan to finish. Cooked tentacles like Fulton's come already tenderized, so you just need to sear and glaze them.

Should I buy fresh or frozen octopus?

Counterintuitively, previously-frozen octopus is often more tender, because freezing helps break down the tough muscle fibers before you ever cook it — many chefs freeze fresh octopus on purpose. So don't treat frozen as second-best here; what matters more is sourcing and honest handling. Buy raw and whole when you want full control, or cooked tentacles when you want a shortcut to dinner.

What does sustainable, traceable octopus actually mean?

It means you can find out how and where the animal was caught, and that the method wasn't destructive. Trap- and pot-caught octopus, like the Seafood Watch-partnered options here, takes the animal with minimal bycatch and no dragging of the seafloor, unlike trawling. A seller that can name the gear and the waters is giving you traceability that the anonymous grocery supply chain simply can't.

How much whole octopus should I buy per person?

Octopus loses a lot of weight as it cooks down, so budget generously — roughly half a pound to three-quarters of a pound of raw whole octopus per person as a main. A two-to-four pound octopus makes a good dinner for a small group. If you're buying already-cooked tentacles, you can plan closer to actual serving weight since the shrinkage has already happened.
