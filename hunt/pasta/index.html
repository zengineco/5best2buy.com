5 Artisan Pasta Makers Worth the Hunt

Supermarket pasta is extruded fast through Teflon dies and flash-dried, which leaves it slick and bland. Artisan pasta is pushed through bronze dies that give it a rough, sauce-grabbing surface, then dried slowly at low temperature — often from heritage or organic grains. The difference in the bowl is real. The makers below are small or import direct. Here's where to get pasta worth cooking.

Straight Answers
Artisan Pasta FAQ
What is bronze-die pasta and why does it matter?

The die is the mold the dough is pushed through to form each shape. A bronze die drags on the dough and leaves a rough, porous surface, which is what lets sauce cling instead of sliding off. Supermarket pasta is extruded through Teflon dies for speed, giving a slick, sealed surface. That single difference, rough versus slick, is the biggest reason artisan pasta feels and tastes better in the bowl.

Why does slow drying make better pasta?

After extrusion the pasta has to dry, and mass producers flash-dry at high heat in hours to move volume, which can scorch the wheat and leave it bland. Artisan makers dry slowly at low temperature over many hours or days, which preserves the grain flavor and gives the noodle a firmer bite. Bronze dies and slow drying usually go together, and both are why a good dried pasta tastes of wheat rather than of nothing.

What is durum semolina and are heritage grains worth it?

Durum is a hard wheat, and its coarse-ground flour, semolina, is the traditional grain for dried pasta because it holds shape and cooks to a firm al dente. Heritage and ancient grains go a step further; varieties like the Sicilian Russello and Perciasacchi that Bona Furtuna uses carry more flavor and a different character than modern commodity durum. They cost more and are grown in small volume, which is exactly why you find them at small makers rather than in the pasta aisle.

How do you cook artisan pasta so the texture shows?

Salt the water well, cook a minute or two short of the package time, and taste for a firm al dente, since good bronze-die pasta has real bite worth keeping. Reserve some starchy cooking water and finish the pasta in the pan with your sauce so the rough surface grabs it. Skip rinsing, which strips the starch that helps sauce cling. The texture you paid for shows up in that last step in the pan, not in the pot.
