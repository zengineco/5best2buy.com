Straight Answers
Rabbit & Small-Pet Food FAQ
How much hay should a rabbit or guinea pig eat?

Unlimited grass hay is the foundation and should make up the large majority of the diet, ideally a pile at least the size of the animal available at all times. The constant chewing wears down teeth that never stop growing and keeps the gut moving, which is literally life-or-death for these animals. Pellets and fresh greens are supplements around the hay, not the main event.

What is wrong with muesli-style rabbit mixes?

Muesli mixes, the colorful blends of flakes, peas, and seeds, invite selective feeding: the animal picks out the sugary, starchy bits and leaves the fibrous pellets. That drives dental disease, obesity, and dangerous gut slowdowns. A plain, uniform, timothy-based pellet where every piece is identical prevents the picking, which is why exotic vets steer owners away from muesli entirely.

Timothy or alfalfa hay?

For healthy adult rabbits and guinea pigs, timothy or another grass hay is right because it is high in fiber and low in calcium and protein. Alfalfa is a legume, not a grass, and its high calcium and calories suit growing babies, pregnant animals, or a vet-directed recovery, but fed to adults it contributes to obesity and bladder-stone risk. When in doubt for a grown animal, choose grass hay.

Do guinea pigs need something rabbits do not?

Yes, vitamin C. Guinea pigs cannot make their own vitamin C and must get it from their diet, so their pellets are fortified with it and they benefit from vitamin-C-rich vegetables like bell pepper. Rabbits produce their own, so rabbit pellets are not a substitute for guinea pig pellets. Never feed one species the other's fortified food as its staple.
