Reptile Food FAQ
Why does the diet depend so much on the species?

Reptiles range from strict carnivores to insectivores to omnivores to near-herbivores, and feeding the wrong category causes real disease. An adult bearded dragon is an omnivore that shifts toward more vegetables with age, a leopard gecko is a strict insectivore, and a crested gecko thrives on a fruit-and-insect complete diet. Always research your exact species before buying food, because there is no single reptile diet.

What is gut-loading and why does it matter?

Gut-loading means feeding your feeder insects a nutritious diet for 24 to 48 hours before offering them, so the cricket or roach becomes a vitamin-rich package rather than an empty one. A hungry, unfed insect passes almost no nutrition to your reptile. Feeding crickets, dubia roaches, or worms quality greens and commercial gut-load turns them into genuinely valuable prey.

Why do I need calcium and D3 supplements?

Insects are naturally low in calcium and have a poor calcium-to-phosphorus ratio, so insectivorous and omnivorous reptiles need feeders dusted with calcium powder to prevent metabolic bone disease, a common and crippling deficiency. Vitamin D3 is needed to absorb that calcium: reptiles make it from proper UVB lighting, and animals without adequate UVB usually need a D3-containing supplement. Balance matters, since over-supplementing certain vitamins is also harmful.

Can a complete gel or powdered diet replace live feeders?

For many species, yes. Complete diets like gel formulas for bearded dragons or fruit-and-insect powders for crested geckos are formulated to be nutritionally complete, and they remove the mess and supplementing guesswork of live feeding. That said, some insectivores still benefit from live prey for enrichment and natural hunting behavior, and a few species do best on live feeders as the core. Match the format to what your species actually needs.
