Farmer & Pot Cheese Worth the Hunt

Farmer cheese and pot cheese are fresh, unaged cheese — cultured curd drained until it's firm enough to crumble or slice. It's the backbone of pierogi, blintzes, kugel, and a slice of rye, and it's nearly vanished from American groceries as the aisle chased shelf-stable process cheese. These makers still press real curd, several of them on recipes older than most brands.

Straight Answers
Farmer & Pot Cheese FAQ
What's the difference between farmer cheese and pot cheese?

Both are fresh, unaged cheese made by draining cultured or acidified curd. Pot cheese is drained less, so it stays wetter and looser, like a dry cottage cheese. Farmer cheese is pressed drier and firm enough to slice or crumble. Cottage cheese is the same family, just left in a creamy dressing.

How do I use farmer cheese?

Crumble it into pierogi, blintzes, and kugel; spread it on rye with tomato; fold it into scrambled eggs or pancakes; or sweeten it with honey and fruit. Eastern European and Baltic cooking lean on it hard. It doesn't melt smooth, so it's for fillings and crumbling, not a grilled-cheese melt.

Is farmer cheese healthy?

Plain farmer cheese is high in protein and low in fat and sugar, and because it's unaged it doesn't carry the heavy salt of aged cheese. The clean-label ones here skip gums and stabilizers, so what you're eating is essentially milk, culture, and a little salt.

How long does it keep?

It's a fresh cheese, so plan on about one to two weeks refrigerated, and use it while it still smells clean and milky. It freezes acceptably if you're going to cook it into a filling, though the texture softens, so it's best fresh.
