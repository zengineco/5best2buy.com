Straight Answers
Pecans FAQ
How should I store pecans so they do not go rancid?

Pecans are high in oil and turn rancid faster than most nuts, so cool storage is not optional if you want to keep them a while. In an airtight container they hold a few months in the pantry, up to about a year in the fridge, and two years or more in the freezer with no loss of quality. Keep them away from strong-smelling foods, because they readily absorb odors. That bitter, paint-like taste in old pecans is the oil having turned.

Should I buy halves or pieces?

They are the same nut graded by size and appearance, so it comes down to use. Halves look best where the pecan is on display — a pecan pie top, a cheese board, or spiced and eaten by hand. Pieces cost less and are the practical choice for baking, stuffing, granola, or anything where they get chopped anyway. Buying pieces for a recipe that hides them is an easy way to save without losing a thing.

Why does fresh-crop matter for pecans specifically?

Because the oils turn quickly, a pecan's freshness is most of its flavor. New-season nuts shipped soon after the fall harvest are sweet and buttery, while commodity pecans pooled and warehoused for a year lose that sweetness and drift toward bitter. Buying direct from the orchard that grew them shortens the time from tree to table, which is the whole point. Fall and early winter are when the freshest crop ships.

Do I need to toast pecans before baking with them?

You do not have to, but a few minutes in a dry pan or a low oven deepens their flavor considerably, coaxing out the nuttiness and adding a little crunch. It is especially worth it for pies, salads, and anything where the pecan is meant to stand out. Watch them closely, because their high oil content means they go from toasted to burnt fast. With genuinely fresh nuts, even untoasted, the raw flavor already carries.
