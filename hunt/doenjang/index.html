Doenjang Worth the Hunt

Doenjang is Korea's deep, funky fermented soybean paste — the backbone of doenjang jjigae and the savory half of ssamjang. The mass-market tubs are fermented fast in a factory and taste flat and salty; the real thing is aged for a year or more in earthenware crocks and tastes like miso's older, wilder cousin. This is a small, import-heavy shelf — a handful of genuine artisan makers reach the US, and we'd rather show you four real ones than pad it.

Straight Answers
Doenjang FAQ
What's the difference between doenjang and miso?

Both are fermented soybean pastes, but doenjang is Korean and typically made from just soybeans and salt with a wilder, funkier, more assertive flavor, while Japanese miso usually adds a rice or barley koji and lands sweeter and smoother. Doenjang is also often chunkier. If you know miso, think of doenjang as its bolder, saltier relative — you can't cleanly swap one for the other.

Why is real doenjang so hard to find in the US?

Traditional doenjang is aged a year or more in earthenware crocks, which is slow, space-hungry, and made in small batches by family fermentaries in Korea. Most of what reaches the US at scale is the fast, factory-fermented version. The genuine long-aged stuff arrives only through specialty importers, which is why this shelf is short and a little pricey.

How do I use doenjang?

It's the base of doenjang jjigae, a hearty Korean stew with tofu, vegetables, and often anchovy or beef, and it's the savory half of ssamjang, the dip for Korean BBQ lettuce wraps. A spoonful also deepens marinades, soups, and dressings the way miso does. Start with less than you think — good doenjang is intense and salty.

Does doenjang need to be refrigerated?

Yes, once opened — it's a live fermented product and keeps best cold, where it'll last a long time and may keep slowly deepening in flavor. Unopened, the salt and fermentation make it very stable, so it ships fine at room temperature. Keep the surface pressed down and covered to avoid drying out.
