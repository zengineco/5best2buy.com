Straight Answers
Bird Food FAQ
Is an all-seed diet really bad for parrots?

Yes. A loose seed mix lets a bird pick out the fatty favorites (sunflower, safflower, peanuts) and leave the rest, so it ends up on a high-fat, nutrient-poor diet that drives obesity and fatty liver disease. Seed also lacks vitamin A and calcium, two of the most common deficiencies avian vets see. Seed is fine as an occasional treat or foraging reward, not the daily meal.

Should I feed pellets or a seed mix?

For most parrots and companion birds, a formulated pellet is the better base because every bite carries the same balanced nutrition, so the bird cannot selectively eat only the fatty pieces. A good diet is roughly pellets plus fresh vegetables and some fruit, with seed as a minor treat. Finches and canaries are more seed-oriented than parrots, so match the diet to the species.

How do I switch a seed junkie onto pellets?

Convert slowly. Birds are neophobic and may not recognize pellets as food at first, so mix a small amount into the familiar seed and gradually shift the ratio over one to several weeks. Offer pellets warm or alongside a favorite veg to make them interesting, and monitor droppings and weight so the bird is actually eating, not starving. Never crash-convert a bird cold turkey.

What foods are toxic to pet birds?

Avocado, chocolate, caffeine, alcohol, onion and garlic, and anything with xylitol are dangerous to birds and should never be offered. Fruit pits and apple seeds contain trace cyanogenic compounds, so core fruit first. Separately, the fumes from overheated nonstick (PTFE) cookware can kill a bird in minutes, so keep birds well away from the kitchen when those pans are in use.
