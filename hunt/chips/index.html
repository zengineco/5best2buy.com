Straight Answers
Chips FAQ
Why does the frying oil matter so much?

Most big-bag chips are fried in cheap, heavily refined seed oils chosen for price, not flavor. Small makers who fry in beef tallow, avocado oil, or other clean oils are making a more expensive choice that shows up in taste and in what you are eating — tallow-fried chips in particular are richer and sturdier. The oil is often the single biggest difference between a mass chip and a craft one. It is worth reading the bag for the specific oil used.

What is a kettle chip, and is it actually different?

Kettle chips are cooked in small batches in a kettle rather than run continuously through a long fryer, so the slices cook unevenly and come out harder, crunchier, and more golden. Continuous-fry chips are thinner and more uniform by design. The batch process is slower and more hands-on, which is why kettle chips are a natural fit for small regional makers. If you want crunch and character over a light, uniform crisp, kettle is the style.

What does heirloom corn get you in a tortilla chip?

Heirloom varieties like Tarahumara blue corn are older, non-commodity strains grown for flavor and tradition rather than industrial yield, so they carry a deeper, nuttier taste and often a story about the community that grows the seed. Standard chips use commodity dent corn bred for volume. You taste the difference most in a simple, lightly-salted chip where the corn is the whole point. It is also a way to support seed and grower traditions that commodity farming erases.

How do I keep chips fresh once the bag is open?

Chips go stale from air and humidity, not from sitting out a day or two, so the fix is sealing them tight. Roll the bag down and clip it, or move them to an airtight container, and keep them away from heat and the moisture near a stove or sink. Do not refrigerate them — the fridge is humid and will make them soft and pick up odors. Small-batch chips have no anti-staling additives, so they reward being sealed up promptly.
