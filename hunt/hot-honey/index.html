Hot honey is everywhere now, and most of the grocery versions are corn syrup with a little heat thrown in. It belongs on pizza, fried chicken, biscuits, sharp cheese, and about half the other things in your kitchen. Here's where to get the real stuff.

Straight Answers
Hot Honey FAQ
How do you actually use hot honey?

The classic move is a drizzle over pizza — especially pepperoni or a salty-sweet white pie — and over fried chicken or hot biscuits. It also shines on sharp aged cheese, goat cheese, roasted carrots, cornbread, and a bourbon cocktail. Because it is thinner and spicier than plain honey, add it at the end as a finisher rather than cooking it down.

Does hot honey crystallize, and is that a problem?

Yes — real raw honey naturally crystallizes over time, and hot honey made from raw honey does the same. It is not spoilage and does not mean anything is wrong; it is a sign the honey was not heavily processed. To bring it back to liquid, stand the jar in a bowl of warm water for a few minutes, and avoid the microwave, which can scorch the sugars and drive off the delicate flavor.

What gives one hot honey more heat than another?

The pepper is the biggest variable. Chili flakes or habanero give a sharper, more upfront burn, while a less-common pepper like the datil brings a slower, fruitier build. Some makers also add a splash of vinegar to balance the sweetness and brighten the finish, which changes how hot it reads on the tongue even at the same pepper level.

Does hot honey need refrigeration, and does it expire?

No — honey is naturally antimicrobial, so hot honey keeps for a very long time at room temperature and should be stored in the pantry, not the fridge, where the cold speeds up crystallizing. Keep the lid clean and the neck wiped so it pours freely. If the honey ever tastes fermented or fizzy, moisture likely got in, but that is rare with a well-sealed jar.
