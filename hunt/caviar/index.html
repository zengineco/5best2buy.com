5 Sustainable Caviar & Roe Sources Worth the Hunt

Caviar has an ugly history: wild beluga and other sturgeon were fished to the edge of extinction for it, and a lot of "caviar" still trades on that legacy. It doesn't have to. America now has excellent sustainable roe — sturgeon raised on land-based farms, and paddlefish responsibly harvested under strict management — that's every bit as good and harms nothing endangered. The sources below are the real, clean ones. Here's where to buy a luxury you can feel fine about.

Straight Answers
Caviar & Roe FAQ
Is farmed caviar as good as wild, and is it more sustainable?

Yes on both counts. Wild sturgeon were fished to the brink for beluga, sevruga, and osetra, which is why buying wild is an ecological problem — land-based farmed sturgeon sidesteps that entirely and is rated a Seafood Watch Best Choice. Modern farmed osetra and white sturgeon caviar rivals the old wild product in quality, and because the farm controls diet and harvest, it's consistent year to year in a way wild never was.

What's the difference between sturgeon caviar and paddlefish or hackleback roe?

Technically only sturgeon roe is "caviar," and farmed sturgeon gives you the classic buttery, nutty pearls. Paddlefish and hackleback are American river fish whose roe is smaller, a touch earthier, and far more affordable — and, when harvested under strict management, genuinely sustainable rather than endangered. They're the smart everyday entry to real roe; save farmed sturgeon for when you want the full caviar experience.

How do I serve caviar properly?

Keep it simple so the roe is the star: serve it well-chilled, ideally over ice, with a non-metal spoon — mother-of-pearl, bone, or plastic — because metal gives it a tinny taste. Classic accompaniments are blini or unsalted toast points with crème fraîche, and traditionalists eat the best tins with nothing at all. Open the tin only just before serving.

How should I store caviar and how long does it keep?

Caviar is highly perishable and lives in the coldest part of your fridge, around the freezing point — many people nestle the tin on ice in the back of the fridge. Unopened, a fresh tin keeps a few weeks up to its date; once opened, eat it within two or three days. Don't freeze fresh caviar, since freezing ruptures the delicate eggs and turns them mushy.
