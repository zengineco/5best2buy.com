Layer Cake & Bundt FAQ
How does a whole cake survive shipping?

Layer cakes ship frozen, packed with dry ice or heavy gel packs in an insulated box, usually sent early in the week so they don't sit over a weekend. They arrive frozen or very cold; you move the cake to the fridge and let it thaw slowly, then bring it to room temperature before serving so the crumb and frosting soften. Freezing actually protects the texture in transit.

Why are these cakes made with so many thin layers?

Thin layers mean more frosting-to-cake ratio in every bite and a more even, moist result, since thin rounds bake through without drying. Smith Island cake takes it to an extreme with eight to ten layers. It's slower and more hands-on than a two-layer cake, which is exactly why the mass market doesn't do it and small bakeries own the style.

How far ahead should I order for an event?

Give yourself a cushion — most makers ship frozen on set days and transit takes a few days, so order a week or more before you need it, especially around holidays when they sell out. Plan for a day of thawing in the fridge once it arrives. Ordering early is the single best way to avoid a last-minute scramble.

What's the real difference from a supermarket cake?

Grocery cakes are typically oil-based sponge with a shortening buttercream engineered to hold up in a display case, which is why they taste sweet but flat and coat your mouth. These are butter- and egg-rich cakes with real fillings — cooked caramel, cream cheese, fudge — built by hand. You're paying for ingredients and labor, not shelf life.
