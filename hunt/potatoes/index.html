Straight Answers
Heirloom Potatoes FAQ
What is the difference between waxy and floury potatoes?

Waxy potatoes (fingerlings, most reds, new potatoes) are low in starch and high in moisture, so they hold their shape — ideal for boiling, roasting, potato salad, and gratins. Floury or starchy ones (russets, many heirlooms, German Butterball) fall apart when cooked, which is what you want for fluffy mash and light fries. Matching the variety to the dish matters more than the brand.

How should I store potatoes so they last?

Keep them cool, dark, and airy — a paper bag or basket in a pantry, never a sealed plastic bag, which traps moisture and rots them. Do not refrigerate: cold converts their starch to sugar and makes them sweet and gritty when cooked. Keep them away from onions, which speed each other's sprouting, and store away from light to prevent greening.

My potatoes turned green or sprouted — are they safe?

Green skin and sprouts signal solanine, a natural compound that is mildly toxic in quantity and tastes bitter. A firm potato with a few small sprouts is fine once you cut the sprouts and any green flesh well away. But if it is heavily green, deeply sprouted, soft, or shriveled, throw it out — it is past the point of being worth eating.

Why is it so hard to buy heirloom potatoes shipped fresh?

Potatoes are cheap and heavy, so the shipping cost often exceeds the value of the spuds — which is why almost no one ships fresh eating potatoes and the grocery bag settles for a few storage varieties. A farm that ships heirlooms anyway is offering texture, color, and flavor you simply cannot get bagged, and charging for the real cost of getting it to your door.
