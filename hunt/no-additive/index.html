Straight Answers
No-Additive FAQ
What counts as an additive I should avoid?

On this shelf, an additive is anything in a product to serve the manufacturer rather than you: chemical preservatives, artificial colors and flavors, thickening gums, emulsifiers, and cheap seed-oil or sugar fillers that pad the recipe. Not every added ingredient is sinister, but a short list of recognizable foods is the signal we look for. The test is whether you can read the whole label in one breath and know what each thing is.

How do I actually read an ingredient list?

Ingredients are listed by weight from most to least, so the first few tell you what the product mostly is. Watch the tail end for the additives: long chemical names, numbered colors, and vague terms like natural flavor. A useful rule of thumb is that a shorter list of things you recognize as food usually beats a long list you cannot pronounce.

Are natural flavors and preservatives really a problem?

Not always dangerous, but often a dodge. Natural flavor is a broad regulatory catch-all that can hide a complex proprietary mix, which defeats the point of a transparent label. Common preservatives like BHA, BHT, and sodium benzoate, and colors like Red 40 or Yellow 5, are the kind of extras this shelf's makers deliberately leave out. The goal is not fear, it is knowing exactly what you are eating.

Do short-ingredient foods spoil faster?

Sometimes, yes, and that is a feature, not a flaw. Without chemical preservatives, some of these products have a shorter shelf life or want refrigeration after opening, which is the honest trade for leaving the additives out. Check storage guidance and buy what you will use, rather than expecting a whole-food product to sit for a year like a heavily preserved one.
