Straight Answers
Regenerative Meat FAQ
What does "regenerative" actually mean for meat?

Regenerative agriculture goes a step beyond organic or grass-fed: it's a set of practices — rotational grazing, keeping soil covered, integrating animals into the land — aimed at rebuilding topsoil, holding water, and pulling carbon back into the ground. The animals are a tool for healing the pasture rather than a load the land merely tolerates. Because "regenerative" isn't a single legal label, look for farms that publish their methods or carry a verification like Land to Market.

Is grass-fed the same as grass-finished?

No, and the distinction matters. "Grass-fed" can legally apply to cattle that ate grass early on but were finished on grain in a feedlot. "Grass-finished" means the animal ate forage its entire life, right up to slaughter. If you want the leaner profile and different fat composition people seek from pasture-raised beef, look specifically for "100% grass-fed and grass-finished."

Why does regenerative and pasture-raised meat cost more?

It takes more land, more time, and more labor: animals grow slower on pasture, farms carry fewer of them per acre, and the whole system is managed by hand rather than optimized in a feedlot. You're also often buying directly from the farm with real cold-chain shipping instead of through a consolidated commodity supply chain. The price reflects the actual cost of raising animals this way — the cheap supermarket number is the one that hides its true costs.

How is direct-shipped frozen meat handled, and how do I store it?

Most regenerative farms flash-freeze cuts at peak freshness and ship them frozen on dry ice or gel packs, which locks in quality better than "fresh" meat that has traveled for days. When a box arrives, move anything still frozen straight to the freezer, where it keeps for several months to a year depending on the cut. Thaw in the refrigerator rather than on the counter, and cook within a day or two once thawed.
