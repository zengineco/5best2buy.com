Craft Butter Makers Worth the Hunt

Butter is not one thing. There's organic European-style sweet cream, churned high in fat; there's grass-fed butter, deep-yellow from what the cows ate; there's slow-churned old-family butter, and there's goat's-milk butter, which is its own bright, tangy animal. Commodity butter flattens all of that into one 80%-fat block built for a price. The makers below keep the differences alive and ship them cold. Match the butter to what you're making.

Straight Answers
Butter FAQ
What makes European-style butter different?

European-style butter is churned to a higher butterfat content — typically 82% or more, versus the 80% minimum that defines standard American butter. That extra fat and lower water content give it a richer mouthfeel, a firmer body, and better browning, which is why it's prized for laminated pastry and for eating plain on bread. It is not the same as cultured butter, though many European-style butters happen to also be cultured.

Why is some butter deep yellow and some nearly white?

Color comes almost entirely from the animal's diet. Cows on green pasture eat beta-carotene-rich grass, which carries through to the fat and produces that deep golden color — so grass-fed butter is naturally yellower and shifts with the seasons. Goat's-milk butter is naturally white because goats convert beta-carotene into colorless vitamin A. Commodity butter is often color-corrected to stay uniform year-round.

Should I buy salted or unsalted butter?

Use unsalted for baking, where you want to control the salt yourself and where salt levels vary between brands. Salted butter is the better choice for the table and for finishing, and the salt also extends its fridge life. Many of the makers here offer both, so buy unsalted if you bake and want one butter to do everything.

How should I store butter to keep it fresh?

Butter picks up fridge odors and goes rancid as its fat oxidizes with light and air, so keep it wrapped and in the covered dairy compartment, not the open door. For long storage, butter freezes extremely well for several months with no loss of quality — wrap it airtight and thaw in the fridge. Fresh small-batch butter has a shorter shelf life than commodity butter precisely because it isn't built to sit.
