Straight Answers
Bagel FAQ
What makes a real bagel different from a grocery-store bagel?

A real bagel is kettle-boiled before it goes in the oven, which sets a chewy interior and a glossy, blistered crust. Most supermarket bagels are steamed instead of boiled, which is faster and cheaper but produces the soft, bready puck people mistake for the real thing. Hand-rolling gives the ring its density and structure, where mass production stamps or extrudes the dough. The boil is the whole difference.

How do you store and reheat bagels that ship frozen?

Keep them frozen until you want one, since freezing right after baking is exactly how these shops preserve the fresh texture. Don't thaw the whole batch on the counter, where they go stale fast. Toast or reheat straight from frozen, halved if you like, and they come back close to fresh out of the oven. A frozen bagel toasted to order beats a day-old fresh one.

What is the difference between a New York and a Montreal bagel?

A New York bagel is boiled in plain or malted water, is larger, and has a softer, saltier crumb. A Montreal bagel is boiled in honey-sweetened water and baked in a wood-fired oven, making it denser, sweeter, and chewier with a thinner ring. St-Viateur is the Montreal benchmark; Boichik and the Long Island and Brooklyn shops here work the New York style. Shipping both frozen lets you settle the argument at your own table.

Are shipped bagels as good as fresh from the counter?

When a shop bakes and freezes at peak and you toast from frozen, they are remarkably close, because the freeze locks in the crust and chew. What you lose is the warm just-baked moment, not the quality of the bagel itself. It beats any bagel trucked to a distant grocery shelf and sitting for days. For anyone outside a real bagel city, shipped-frozen is the honest way to eat the genuine article.
