Lollipops & Hard Candy FAQ
How is hard candy actually made?

Sugar and corn syrup (or just sugar) are cooked to around 300°F, the hard-crack stage, then flavored, colored, and shaped as they cool. Craft makers pull and fold the hot sugar by hand to build color patterns or even whole images, then cut or drop it into shape. Once it cools it sets glassy and hard.

Why is handmade hard candy worth more than a bag of drops?

You're paying for real flavor and hand labor instead of volume. Craft makers use actual fruit and botanical flavors (and sometimes organic or dye-free ingredients) and shape each batch by hand, which is slow. Mass hard candy is optimized to be made by the ton for pennies, and it tastes like it.

Does hard candy expire?

It lasts a very long time — often a year or more — because it's basically pure sugar with almost no moisture for spoilage. Its enemy is humidity: exposed to damp air it gets sticky and cloudy. Keep it individually wrapped or sealed airtight and it'll stay clear and crisp.

Why do some hard candies get sticky or cloudy?

That's the sugar absorbing moisture from the air, which starts to recrystallize the surface. It's a sign the candy wasn't kept sealed, not that it's unsafe. Individually wrapped pieces hold up best; for loose candy, an airtight jar with the lid firmly on does the job.
