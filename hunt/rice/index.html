5 Heritage Rice & Grain Growers Worth the Hunt

Most rice and grain in the store is a commodity — anonymous, grown for yield, milled who-knows-when. Heritage grain is the opposite: named varieties with real history and real flavor, grown by small farms and often milled to order so it reaches you fresh. Carolina Gold rice, Sea Island grits, hand-harvested Ojibwe wild rice — these are foods with a place and a story. Here's where to buy them from the people who grow them.

Two Brooks grows, mills, and packs single-estate rice on one Mississippi Delta farm ten generations deep — white, brown, jasmine, basmati, red, and black, plus rice grits and flour. "Single-estate" means every grain comes from the same land, the way you'd talk about a wine or an olive oil. It's rice with an actual address, from a family that's farmed it for generations. A revelation if you've only ever bought rice by the anonymous bag.

No.4 · The Indigenous Harvest

Native Harvest

White Earth Nation, MN · hand-harvested wild rice

Native Harvest, a project of the White Earth Land Recovery Project, sells hand-harvested Ojibwe wild rice — manoomin, the "good berry" that's central to Anishinaabe foodways. This is true wild rice, gathered by canoe the traditional way, not the cultivated paddy rice sold as "wild" in stores; the difference in flavor and texture is profound. Buying it supports Indigenous food sovereignty directly. The most meaningful grain on this shelf.

Straight Answers
Heritage Rice and Grain FAQ
What makes heritage rice different from a supermarket bag?

Commodity rice is grown for yield, blended across farms, and often milled long before it reaches you, so it arrives anonymous and flat. Heritage rice is a named variety with real history, like Carolina Gold, grown by a specific farm and frequently milled close to when you order, which is why Anson Mills cold-mills to order. Fresher milling means more aroma and flavor, and single-estate growers like Two Brooks give the grain an actual address.

Which rice variety should you use for what?

Match the grain to the dish. Long-grain aromatics like jasmine and basmati stay separate and fluffy for pilafs and everyday sides; short and medium grains turn creamy and are better for risotto-style dishes and puddings. Carolina Gold is a fragrant heritage long-grain prized in Lowcountry cooking, and true hand-harvested wild rice is a grass seed, nutty and chewy, best as a side or in a blend rather than a swap for white rice.

Is hand-harvested wild rice different from the wild rice in stores?

Yes, substantially. Most rice labeled wild in stores is paddy-cultivated and machine-harvested, uniform and very dark. True wild rice like the Ojibwe manoomin from Native Harvest is hand-gathered by canoe from natural waters, with a lighter, more varied grain and a distinctly nutty, tender character. It is a limited seasonal harvest tied to a community, which is why it costs more and tastes nothing like the cultivated version.

How should you store rice, grits, and whole grains?

White rice is stable for a long time in an airtight container away from heat and moisture. Brown rice, fresh grits, and whole grains still carry their oil-rich bran, so they go rancid within months and are best kept cool, in the fridge or freezer for longer holding. Because heritage grain is often freshly milled, buy amounts you will use in a season rather than stockpiling, and let smell be your guide to freshness.
