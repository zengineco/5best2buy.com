Scallops Worth the Hunt

Most 'fresh' scallops are 'wet' — soaked in a phosphate solution (STP) that pumps them full of water so they weigh more and steam instead of sear. Dry, chemical-free scallops from day-boats are a different animal: they brown hard and taste of the sea. These independents ship them dry.

Straight Answers
Scallops FAQ
What's the difference between 'dry' and 'wet' scallops?

Wet scallops are soaked in a sodium tripolyphosphate (STP) solution that makes them absorb water, so they weigh more and look bright white. In the pan that water floods out and they steam grey instead of searing. Dry scallops have no additive — they brown hard and taste sweeter. Every maker here sells dry.

Are frozen scallops worse than fresh?

Not if they're flash-frozen dry at the peak, like Wulf's. A scallop frozen hours off the boat can be fresher than a 'fresh' one that traveled for days. What matters far more is dry-vs-wet and how fast it was frozen, not fresh-vs-frozen.

When is scallop season?

Maine's inshore dayboat scallop season runs roughly December through early April, which is why Downeast Dayboat sells only then. Georges Bank scallops (Wulf's) are landed and frozen year-round. If you want the freshest inshore product, buy in winter.

How should I cook a dry scallop?

Pat it bone-dry, salt it, and sear in a screaming-hot pan with a little oil, undisturbed, until a deep crust forms — then flip for a few seconds. Because there's no injected water, it browns instead of weeping. Don't crowd the pan.
