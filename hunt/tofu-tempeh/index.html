Straight Answers
Tofu & Tempeh FAQ
What is the difference between tofu and tempeh?

Tofu is made by curdling fresh soy milk and pressing the curds into blocks, so it's smooth and mild and takes on whatever flavor you cook it with. Tempeh is made by fermenting whole cooked soybeans until a firm cake binds together, so it's nuttier, chewier, and higher in protein and fiber because the whole bean is used. Tofu absorbs; tempeh already tastes of something.

Why does organic or non-GMO soy matter here?

Most commodity soybeans in the US are genetically modified and grown with heavy herbicide use, so shoppers who want to avoid that look for organic or non-GMO soy. Beyond the sourcing, the small makers here use identity-preserved beans, sometimes grown on their own farm, which gives the tofu and tempeh a cleaner, fuller bean flavor. It's a distinction the industrial brick usually can't claim.

How do I get the best texture out of craft tofu?

Firmness is the main variable: silken is delicate and best in soups, blended sauces, and desserts, while firm and extra-firm hold up to searing, grilling, and stir-frying. For crisp tofu, press out excess water first, then dry-fry or roast it hot; freezing and thawing tofu makes it spongier and better at soaking up marinade. Craft tofu starts firmer and nuttier than the watery grocery block, so it needs less fussing.

How should I store tofu and tempeh, and can I freeze them?

Keep fresh tofu refrigerated and submerged in water, changing the water daily, and use it within a few days of opening. Tempeh keeps a bit longer refrigerated and both freeze well — many of the makers here ship frozen for exactly that reason. A little harmless white bloom on tempeh is normal fermentation; discard it only if it smells off or shows black or colored mold.
