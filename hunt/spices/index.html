5 Single-Origin Spice Companies Worth the Hunt

Supermarket spices are usually old, anonymous, and ground who-knows-when — a jar of "cinnamon" from no particular place. The companies below do the opposite: they buy single-origin, direct from named farms, and most pay growers far above the commodity rate. The spices are fresher, they taste like something, and you can actually find out where they came from. Several are certified B Corps. Here's where to buy them.

Straight Answers
Spices FAQ
Should I buy whole spices or ground?

Whole spices, wherever you can. A whole spice keeps its volatile aromatic oils sealed inside until you grind it, so it stays potent for years; ground spice starts losing that aroma within months of milling. Toast whole cumin, coriander, or peppercorns and grind them fresh in a cheap electric mill or mortar, and the difference in a dish is immediate.

How long do spices actually last?

They do not spoil, but they fade. As a rough rule: whole spices hold well for two to four years, ground spices for about one year, and dried leafy herbs for under a year. The real test is smell — if it does not hit you when you open the jar, it will not do much in the pot. Single-origin spices bought fresh simply start from a much stronger baseline.

What is the best way to store spices?

Cool, dark, dry, and airtight. The rack mounted next to the stove or above it is the worst spot — heat and steam are exactly what strip flavor. Keep jars in a drawer or a cabinet away from the range, sealed tightly, and buy in amounts you will use within a year rather than warehouse-size tubs.

Why does single-origin spice cost more than the supermarket jar?

Because someone bought it directly from a named farm, often paying growers well above the commodity rate, and shipped it fresh rather than letting it sit in a warehouse for a year or two. You are paying for traceability, freshness, and a fairer price to the farmer — and it shows up as spice that actually tastes of something instead of generic dust.
