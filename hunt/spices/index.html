5 Single-Origin Spice Companies Worth the Hunt

Supermarket spices are usually old, anonymous, and ground who-knows-when — a jar of "cinnamon" from no particular place. The companies below do the opposite: they buy single-origin, direct from named farms, and most pay growers far above the commodity rate. The spices are fresher, they taste like something, and you can actually find out where they came from. Several are certified B Corps. Here's where to buy them.

Published June 2026 · Updated 29 Jun 2026

How this list works. Every company here is small or independent, buys with real sourcing transparency, and earns its spot on merit — nobody pays to be listed. What pushes spices like these off the shelf isn't any one store; it's a commodity system that rewards cheap and old over fresh and traceable. These are the people working against that.
No.1 · The Single-Origin Standard

Burlap & Barrel

New York, NY · Certified B Corp

Burlap & Barrel buys spices single-origin, directly from the smallholder farmers who grow them, and a lot of what they carry barely exists elsewhere — their cinnamon, their saffron, their wild-harvested things. It's a certified B Corp and the sourcing is fully traceable. The gap between a fresh single-origin spice and a years-old supermarket jar is not subtle; you taste it the first time. A good first order is whatever spice you reach for most.

Why it isn't on AmazonThe whole model is direct farmer relationships and small-lot sourcing, which doesn't fit a commodity supply chain. The traceability is the product, and that's the part a generic listing throws away.

No.2 · The Most Transparent in the Category

Diaspora Co.

Single-origin Indian & Sri Lankan spices

Diaspora Co. started with one turmeric and now works with more than 150 farms across India and Sri Lanka, paying growers around six times the commodity price. The spices are grown on regenerative farms, shipped fresh, and traceable down to the named farm. Their turmeric and their pepper are the usual entry points, and they're a genuinely different thing than what you've had. If you care where your food comes from, this is one of the most transparent companies in the whole category.

Why it isn't on AmazonPaying farmers six times the going rate and naming every farm is the opposite of a commodity operation. That model runs on the relationships, not on warehouse volume.

See it at Diaspora Co. →
No.3 · The One With a Mission

Rumi Spice

Afghan saffron & spices · Certified B Corp

Rumi Spice sources saffron directly from farmers in Afghanistan, and the company largely exists to give those farmers a stable, legal income. It's a certified B Corp, and the saffron is high grade — saffron happens to be the one spice where freshness and sourcing show up most obviously. If you've only ever used the tired stuff from a grocery shelf, this will reset what you think saffron tastes like.

Why it isn't on AmazonBuilding a direct supply chain out of Afghan farms is slow, deliberate work, and the mission is the whole point. That's a company doing something hard, not a marketplace seller.

See it at Rumi Spice →
No.4 · The Chef-Built One

Spicewalla

Asheville, NC · founded by chef Meherwan Irani

Spicewalla comes from chef Meherwan Irani, a James Beard nominee, and it's built around freshness: small batches, ground in-house, sealed in tins instead of left to fade on a shelf. The range runs well past a hundred spices and blends, and the blends in particular are good because a working chef designed them. It's the easiest company on this list to just start using across your whole kitchen.

Why it isn't on AmazonThe pitch is freshness and small-batch grinding, which only holds if the company controls the whole chain. A jar that sat in a warehouse for a year defeats the reason to buy it.

See it at Spicewalla →
No.5 · The Quiet Obsessive

Curio Spice Co.

Cambridge, MA · woman-owned, Certified B Corp

Curio is a small, woman-owned B Corp whose founder travels to source spices directly at origin, paying some growers up to ten times the commodity rate. It's the most quietly serious operation here — single-origin, deeply sourced, and a little harder to find, which is exactly why it belongs on this list. Their blends and their vanilla are good places to start.

Why it isn't on AmazonWhen the founder is personally sourcing at origin and paying ten times commodity, the volume is small by design. You find Curio because someone points you to it — not because an algorithm surfaced it.
