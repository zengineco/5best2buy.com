Straight Answers
Mexican Cheese FAQ
What is the difference between cotija, queso fresco, and Oaxaca?

Cotija is aged, dry, salty, and crumbly — a finishing cheese often called a Mexican parmesan. Queso fresco is fresh, mild, soft, and crumbly, with a cooling quality that balances spicy dishes. Oaxaca is a stretched-curd melting cheese similar to mozzarella, used for quesadillas and anywhere you want a good melt.

Which Mexican cheese melts?

Oaxaca and asadero (quesadilla) cheese melt beautifully — they are stretched-curd cheeses made for it. Cotija and queso fresco do not melt; they soften and hold their shape, which is exactly the point when you use them as a crumbled topping.

Why is real artisan Mexican cheese so hard to find?

The US market is dominated by a few large industrial brands, and genuine small creameries making traditional Mexican-style cheeses are rare. That is why this shelf is short and honest — and why we keep spots open for the real makers we want to find.

How do I store and use Mexican cheeses?

Keep them refrigerated and well wrapped. Crumble cotija or queso fresco over finished tacos, tostadas, and beans; shred or tear Oaxaca for melting. Use fresh cheeses within about a week for the best flavor and texture.
