Straight Answers
Small-Batch Candy FAQ
Why do small-batch caramels and toffee use butter instead of corn syrup?

Real caramel and toffee get their flavor from actual butter and cane sugar slowly cooked until they brown and deepen — that is where the rich, almost nutty taste comes from. Mass candy leans on corn syrup and hydrogenated oils because they are cheap, shelf-stable, and forgiving to machine at scale, but they cook up flat and waxy. A slow butter cook is harder and does not keep as long, which is exactly why it tastes better.

How long does small-batch candy stay fresh?

Because it skips preservatives, real caramel and toffee are best within a few weeks to a couple of months. Keep it cool, dry, and airtight — humidity is the enemy, since it makes hard candy sticky and brittle go soft. Brittle and toffee generally hold longest; soft caramels are happiest eaten sooner. Refrigerate only if your kitchen is warm, and bring pieces back to room temperature before eating.

What is the difference between brittle, toffee, and hard candy?

All three are cooked sugar taken to different temperatures. Brittle is cooked to the hard-crack stage with nuts folded in, then spread thin so it snaps. Toffee is cooked with a lot of butter for a richer, sometimes chewier bite, and is often coated in chocolate. Hard candy is sugar cooked hot and flavored but without the butter or nuts, which is why it stays glassy and clear.

Can small candy makers accommodate allergies?

It varies by maker, and it is worth asking directly. A small kitchen that cooks caramels, brittle, and toffee in the same space almost always handles tree nuts, peanuts, dairy, and soy, so cross-contact is likely even on a nut-free item. The upside of buying from a tiny producer is that a real person can tell you exactly what runs through their kitchen — something a mass brand's label rarely makes clear.
