5 Craft Kombuchas Worth the Hunt

Most grocery kombucha is over-sweet and from a brand in every cooler. Real craft kombucha is raw, alive, low-sugar, and made in small batches — the catch is that it's perishable, so few makers ship it. The ones below actually do, cold-packed to your door, including a couple of rarer ferments like Jun and water kefir. Here's where to get the living stuff.

Straight Answers
Kombucha FAQ
What actually is kombucha?

It is sweetened tea fermented by a SCOBY — a symbiotic culture of bacteria and yeast — which eats most of the sugar and produces acids, a light fizz, and a tangy flavor over one to a few weeks. Raw craft kombucha stays unpasteurized so the live cultures remain active, which is why it needs refrigeration. Jun is a close cousin fermented with honey and green tea, and water kefir is a related ferment made with a different culture and no tea at all.

How much sugar and alcohol is in it?

Fermentation consumes most of the sugar the tea started with, so finished craft kombucha is far lower in sugar than soda — though makers vary, and some add juice back for flavor, so it is worth reading the label. The same process creates a trace of alcohol; commercial non-alcoholic kombucha stays under 0.5 percent ABV by law. Makers with a separate hard or alcoholic line, like a spiked Jun, keep it clearly labeled apart from the everyday bottle.

Does it have to stay refrigerated?

Yes — raw, live kombucha is perishable, and warmth lets the culture keep fermenting, which builds pressure, sharpens the sour, and can make bottles gush when opened. Keep it cold from the moment it arrives, which is why the makers here ship it cold-packed in insulated boxes rather than leaving it on a warm truck. Shelf-stable versions exist but have usually been pasteurized, killing the live cultures that are the point.

What are the strands or sediment at the bottom of the bottle?

Those cloudy strands and bits of sediment are pieces of the living culture and yeast — a normal, expected sign that the kombucha is raw and unfiltered rather than a defect. Give the bottle a gentle tip to mix or leave it to settle, whichever you prefer. Perfectly clear, sediment-free kombucha usually means it was heavily filtered or pasteurized.
