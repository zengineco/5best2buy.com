Straight Answers
Specialty Mushroom FAQ
How do I use dried wild mushrooms like porcini or morels?

Rehydrate them first: cover with warm water and soak for 20 to 30 minutes until soft, then lift them out and chop. Crucially, save the soaking liquid — strain out any grit and use it as an intense mushroom stock in risotto, sauces, and soups. Dried mushrooms are more concentrated than fresh, so a small handful goes a long way, and they keep for a very long time in a sealed jar.

How do the grow-your-own kits actually work?

A kit is a block of colonized substrate already full of mushroom mycelium, ready to fruit. You cut the opening, mist it daily to keep humidity up, and within one to two weeks it pushes out a full flush of mushrooms right on your counter — lion's mane, oyster, and the like. Most kits give a couple of flushes before they're spent. It's genuinely easy, which is why they make good gifts and first projects.

What's the best specialty mushroom for a beginner cook?

Oyster mushrooms are the easiest starting point — mild, quick-cooking, forgiving, and both simple to grow and widely sold dried. From there, lion's mane has a seafood-like texture that's a fun step up, while dried porcini and chanterelles add deep savory flavor to sauces and rice dishes. Build from the gentle, versatile varieties toward the more intense wild ones.

How should I store fresh and dried mushrooms?

Keep fresh mushrooms in the fridge in a paper bag, not sealed plastic, so they breathe instead of turning slimy, and use them within several days. Dried mushrooms are the opposite — store them in an airtight container away from light and heat and they last for a year or more. Never soak fresh mushrooms in water to clean them; brush or wipe them so they don't turn soggy in the pan.
