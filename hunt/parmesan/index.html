Parmesan Worth the Hunt

The green can of "parmesan" and a wedge of real Parmigiano-Reggiano share almost nothing but a name. True Parmigiano-Reggiano is DOP-certified, aged 24 months or more, made from raw milk in a small corner of Emilia to rules that haven't changed in centuries — nutty, crystalline, and deeply savory. Grated over pizza or pasta, or eaten in shards, it's transformative. Below are the direct importers who ship the real wheel, plus the American makers doing aged parm honestly.

Straight Answers
Parmesan FAQ
What is the difference between Parmigiano-Reggiano and "parmesan"?

Parmigiano-Reggiano is a DOP-protected cheese made only in a defined zone of Emilia-Romagna, from raw milk, aged at least 12 months and usually 24 or more, to rules that have barely changed in centuries. Every legitimate wheel is pin-dotted with its name across the rind. "Parmesan" is a generic term anyone can use, from honestly-aged American hard cheese to the shelf-stable green-can powder — so the label word tells you little; the certification and the maker tell you everything.

Is American parmesan worth buying instead of the DOP?

Yes, on its own terms. American makers can't legally call their cheese Parmigiano-Reggiano, but the best of them age real hard cheese in the Italian tradition, and some have won top international awards. It's typically more affordable than imported DOP and grates and melts beautifully. Buy the Italian original when you want the crystalline, long-aged experience; buy honest American parm as a superb everyday grating cheese.

How should I store a wedge of parmesan?

Wrap the wedge in parchment or wax paper, then loosely in foil, and keep it in the fridge — that lets the cheese breathe without drying out or sweating the way tight plastic causes. A well-kept wedge lasts for weeks; grate only what you need and re-wrap the rest. If a little surface dries or gets a spot of harmless mold, simply trim it off.

What do I do with the parmesan rind?

Never throw the rind away — it's pure concentrated flavor. Drop it into a simmering soup, stock, risotto, or tomato sauce and let it melt slowly to add deep savory, umami depth, then fish out whatever hasn't dissolved. Rinds keep for months in the freezer, so save them up. Buying a real wedge rather than pre-grated cheese is what gives you the rind in the first place.
