The producers below are actual family sugarhouses — many you can trace right to the trees — making pure syrup the slow way, some of it barrel-aged or infused into things you'll never find in a store. Here's where to get the real thing.

Straight Answers
Maple Syrup FAQ
What do the maple syrup grades mean?

Since 2015 all pure syrup is Grade A, sorted by color and flavor into Golden (Delicate), Amber (Rich), Dark (Robust), and Very Dark (Strong). Golden is light and mild, made early in the season; the darker grades come later and taste more intensely of maple. There is no quality ranking here — it is purely a flavor scale, so pick by whether you want it subtle on pancakes or bold enough to stand up in coffee, baking, or a glaze.

What is the difference between real maple syrup and pancake syrup?

Pure maple syrup has exactly one ingredient: boiled-down sap from a maple tree. "Pancake" or "table" syrup is usually corn syrup or high-fructose corn syrup with caramel color and artificial maple flavoring, often with little or no real maple in it at all. Check the label — if it lists anything besides "maple syrup," it is not the real thing, and the difference in taste is immediate.

How should I store maple syrup, and does it go bad?

An unopened container keeps almost indefinitely in a cool, dark pantry. Once opened, pure syrup must go in the refrigerator, where it lasts about a year, because it has no preservatives. If a little mold ever forms on top, the syrup itself is not ruined — you can skim it, bring the syrup to a boil, and re-jar it, though many people just refrigerate from the start to avoid it.

Why does it take so much sap to make syrup?

It takes roughly 40 gallons of maple sap to produce a single gallon of syrup, because fresh sap is only about two percent sugar and the rest is water that has to be boiled off. That is why real maple is never cheap and why family sugarhouses spend the whole tapping season at the evaporator. Barrel-aged and infused syrups add even more time and craft on top of that already slow process.
