5 Small-Smokehouse Bacon Makers Worth the Hunt

Most grocery bacon is commodity pork, wet-injected with brine and "smoke flavor" to add weight and speed, then sliced thin. The smokehouses below do it the slow way: heritage-breed pork, a real salt cure that takes days, and actual hardwood smoke. It costs more and there's less of it, because dry-curing removes water instead of adding it. Here's where to buy the good stuff.

Straight Answers
Bacon FAQ
What's the difference between dry-cured and wet-cured bacon?

Wet-cured (or wet-injected) bacon is pumped with a brine of salt, sugar and often liquid smoke, which adds water weight and speeds production — it's the grocery default and it shrinks and puddles in the pan. Dry-cured bacon is rubbed with salt and left to cure over days, which draws water out instead of pushing it in. The result is denser, meatier bacon that actually crisps rather than boils in its own released liquid.

Is uncured or nitrate-free bacon actually healthier?

"Uncured" is mostly a labeling rule: instead of added sodium nitrite, these bacons use celery powder or juice, which is naturally high in nitrate that converts to nitrite anyway. So chemically the difference is smaller than the marketing suggests. A traditional cure using measured sodium nitrite is a legitimate, centuries-old method — the more honest question is the quality of the pork and the smoke, not the word on the front of the pack.

Why is thick-cut bacon worth seeking out?

Thin commodity slices are cut that way partly to make a pound look like more bacon and to cook fast in a fryer. Thick-cut, especially from a whole-animal butcher, gives you a meatier bite with rendered-but-not-obliterated fat, and it holds up on a sandwich or in a braise. It rewards low-and-slow cooking, which is where good dry-cured pork really shows its character.

How should I store good bacon to keep it fresh?

Keep an opened pack tightly wrapped in the coldest part of the fridge and use it within about a week, since real dry-cured bacon has fewer preservatives than the commodity kind. For longer storage, freeze it — lay slices flat with parchment between them, or freeze in meal-size portions, and it'll keep for a few months. Thaw in the fridge, not on the counter, to protect both texture and safety.
