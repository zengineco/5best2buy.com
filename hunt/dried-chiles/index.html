Straight Answers
Dried Chiles FAQ
What are the main dried chiles and how hot are they?

Guajillo is mild-to-medium with a bright, tangy flavor — the workhorse of Mexican cooking. Ancho is a dried poblano: mild, with a sweet, raisiny depth. Pasilla is mild, dark, and earthy. Chile de árbol is genuinely hot with a clean heat. Most salsas blend a mild base chile with a little árbol for heat.

How do I use dried chiles?

Toast them briefly on a hot dry comal or skillet until fragrant — don't burn them or they turn bitter. Then soak them in hot water for 15 to 20 minutes to rehydrate, and blend them into a salsa or adobo using the soaking liquid.

Whole dried chiles or chile powder?

Whole pods keep their aromatic oils and give a deeper, fresher flavor, and you control the blend yourself. Powders are convenient but fade fast and are often cut with other spices. For real depth, buy whole pods and grind or blend them as you cook.

How do I store dried chiles?

Keep them airtight, away from light and heat. They'll keep for about a year but are best within six months. A pliable, leathery pod is fresh; a brittle one that snaps is old.
