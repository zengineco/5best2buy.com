5 Artisan Cheeses Worth the Hunt

Grocery "artisan" cheese is usually a factory wheel with a rustic label. The real thing comes from small creameries — often a single farm with its own animals — and a lot of them now ship cold, straight to your door. The makers below are among the best in the country, and a couple are working farmsteads where the cows are visible from the make room. Here's where to order them.

Published June 2026 · Updated 29 Jun 2026

How this list works. Every maker here is a small creamery or a serious independent monger, and earns its spot on merit — nobody pays to be listed. What fills the grocery cheese case isn't one store; it's consolidation that rewards bland, shelf-stable wheels over living, farm-based cheese. These are the people keeping the real thing alive.
No.1 · The Best of American Cheese

Jasper Hill Farm

Greensboro, VT · cave-aged farmstead

Jasper Hill is one of the most respected names in American cheese — a Vermont farmstead with its own underground aging cellars, where they ripen both their own wheels and cheeses from other small makers. The results (the Bayley Hazen Blue, the Harbison) are world-class and regularly hold their own against the best of Europe. If you want to taste the top of what American cheese can do, start here.

Why it isn't on AmazonCave-aging living farmstead wheels is slow work that depends on the specific farm and cellar. That's the opposite of anything a warehouse can stock.

No.2 · The Organic Farmstead

von Trapp Farmstead

Waitsfield, VT · certified organic, raw-milk

von Trapp is a third-generation Vermont farmstead making certified-organic, raw-milk alpine-style cheese from pasture-grazed cows on regenerative land — the herd is genuinely visible from where the cheese is made. It's the ethics standout of the group: organic, raw-milk, and farm-based in the fullest sense. The Oma, a washed-rind, is the one to try first.

Why it isn't on AmazonA single organic farm making raw-milk cheese from its own herd is about as far from a commodity supply chain as food gets. The farm is the product.

See it at von Trapp →
No.3 · The West Coast Benchmark

Cowgirl Creamery

Point Reyes, CA · organic artisan

Cowgirl Creamery helped define West Coast artisan cheese — organic, soft-ripened cheeses like the Mt Tam and Red Hawk that are benchmarks of the style. They ship nationwide and have spent decades treating dairy as something to do thoughtfully rather than cheaply. A great pick when you want soft, bloomy, special-occasion cheese with a real point of view.

Why it isn't on AmazonSoft-ripened organic cheese is perishable, careful, reputation-driven work. The name on it is the whole guarantee, and that doesn't survive a generic listing.

See it at Cowgirl Creamery →
No.4 · The Heartland Family Farm

Marcoot Jersey Creamery

Greenville, IL · 7th-generation, A2 milk

Marcoot is a seventh-generation family Jersey-cow farmstead in Illinois, cave-aging their cheeses and working with A2/A2 milk that's easier on a lot of stomachs. It's the heartland family-farm pick — small, multi-generational, doing the whole thing themselves from the herd up. A genuinely lovely operation to support, and the cheese earns its place on its own.

Why it isn't on AmazonSeven generations on one farm making cave-aged cheese from their own herd is a way of life, not a product line. It was never built for a big platform.

See it at Marcoot →
No.5 · The Expert Who Hunts For You

Saxelby Cheese

New York, NY · curated American farmstead cheese

Saxelby is a small New York monger that has spent years championing American farmstead cheese specifically, curating from small creameries around the country and shipping nationwide. Think of it as the expert who's already done the hunting for you: if you don't know where to start, a Saxelby selection is a guided tour of the best small makers in the country.

Why it isn't on AmazonA monger's whole value is the human taste and the relationships behind the selection — exactly the thing a marketplace strips out.
