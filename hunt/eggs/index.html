Pasture-Raised Eggs Worth the Hunt

"Cage-free" and "free-range" are mostly marketing — the hens still live indoors. True pasture-raised means birds on grass, rotated to fresh ground, eating bugs and forage, and the eggs show it: deep-orange yolks that stand up tall, with a richer flavor that ruins you for the commodity carton. A few farms will ship them to your door on dry ice; more deliver regionally. Either way, once you've had a real one, breakfast changes.

Straight Answers
Pasture-Raised Eggs FAQ
What's the difference between cage-free, free-range, and pasture-raised?

Cage-free only means the hens aren't in cages — they can still be packed indoors by the thousands with no outdoor access. Free-range requires some access to the outdoors, but that can be a small door to a bare porch. Pasture-raised is the meaningful label: hens living on actual grass with room to forage, and the strongest programs require a set amount of outdoor space per bird and rotation to fresh ground.

Why are pasture-raised yolks so deep orange?

The color comes from what the hen eats. Birds foraging on real pasture take in grass, bugs, and carotenoid-rich plants, which deepen the yolk to a rich orange and add flavor — a confined hen on plain grain lays a paler, yellow yolk. It's a visible signal of the diet, which is also why some farms note corn-and-soy-free or non-GMO feed, distinctions that change the egg's fat profile.

Do farm-fresh eggs need to be refrigerated?

In the US, commercially sold eggs are washed, which removes the natural protective bloom on the shell, so those eggs must be kept refrigerated. Some small farms sell unwashed eggs that can sit on the counter for a while, but once any egg has been refrigerated, keep it that way. When in doubt, refrigerate, and store eggs in the body of the fridge rather than the warmer door.

How can I tell if my eggs are still fresh?

Use the float test: place an egg in a bowl of water — a fresh one sinks and lies flat, while an older egg stands upright or floats as the air cell inside grows. A floater is best discarded. Very fresh farm eggs are also famously hard to peel when hard-boiled, so save older eggs for boiling and use the freshest for frying and poaching where the whites hold tight.
