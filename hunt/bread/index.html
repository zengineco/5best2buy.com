Straight Answers
Artisan Bread FAQ
What makes real sourdough different from supermarket bread?

Real sourdough is naturally leavened, raised by a wild yeast and bacteria culture rather than commercial yeast, and given a long fermentation measured in many hours. That slow ferment builds the tang, the open crumb, and a structure many people find easier to digest. Supermarket bread leans on fast commercial yeast plus dough conditioners and preservatives so it can sit for weeks. Four ingredients and a clock versus a label full of additives.

How do bakeries ship fresh bread without it going stale?

They use a few honest tricks. Some par-bake the loaf and ship it partway done so you finish it in your own oven, which is how 123Dough works and why the bread comes out hot and crackling. Others ship the same day they bake, like Simple Kneads, to get the loaf to you at its freshest. Freezing on arrival and reheating is the reliable way to hold quality; a good crust comes back with a short blast in a hot oven.

Can gluten-free bread actually be real sourdough?

Yes, though it is hard to do well. Genuinely gluten-free sourdough is naturally leavened with a wild culture rather than propped up entirely by gums, which is what gives it real tang and chew instead of the crumbly texture that gives gluten-free bread its reputation. Bread SRSLY and Simple Kneads both bake naturally cultured gluten-free loaves. For anyone who gave up on real bread, it is a genuine option, not a compromise.

How should you store artisan bread to keep it fresh?

Because real bread has no preservatives, it lives on a short clock. Keep a loaf you are eating within a day or two at room temperature, cut-side down or in paper, never sealed in plastic where the crust goes soft. For longer keeping, slice and freeze, then toast or reheat straight from frozen. Do not refrigerate bread; the fridge is the fastest way to make it stale.
