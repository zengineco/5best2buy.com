5 Artisan Mustard Makers Worth the Hunt

Mustard is one of the cheapest things in the store, which is exactly why the mass versions are so dull — bright yellow, one note, made to disappear into a hot dog. Real mustard is alive: stone-ground from whole seeds, sharp and complex, sometimes mixed with beer or horseradish or maple. The makers below grind in small batches, and one of them is the last traditional mustard mill of its kind in North America. Here's where to buy them.

Straight Answers
Mustard FAQ
What's the difference between Dijon, whole-grain, and yellow mustard?

Yellow mustard is mild and tangy, made from ground yellow seed with turmeric for its bright color and vinegar for tang. Dijon uses brown or black seed and traditionally white wine or verjus, giving a sharper, more pungent bite with a smooth texture. Whole-grain (or stone-ground) leaves the seeds partly intact, so you get bursts of texture and a slower, rounder heat — the style most of the small mills here specialize in.

Why does some mustard taste so much sharper than others?

Mustard's heat comes from a chemical reaction when the crushed seed meets liquid, and cold water builds the most pungency while hot liquid and vinegar tame it. Freshly ground seed, like the cold stone-milling Raye's does, keeps those volatile oils intact, so the mustard hits sharper than a mass product ground fast and hot. The heat also mellows as a jar ages, which is why fresh small-batch mustard has more punch.

Does mustard need to be refrigerated, and does it go bad?

Mustard is high in acid and salt, so it is remarkably shelf-stable and will not spoil in the dangerous sense for a long time. Refrigerating after opening mainly preserves flavor and pungency, which fade at room temperature; whole-grain and wine-based mustards especially keep their edge better cold. It may darken or dry on top over months — stir it back in — but the acidity keeps it safe well past when it tastes its best.

What do you do with the specialty mustards — beer, horseradish, maple?

Beer and ale mustards, like a whole-grain golden ale style, are built for sausages, pretzels, and ham. Horseradish or "Drunken" mustards cut through rich roast beef and sharp cheddar, while sweeter maple or fig mustards play against pork, in a vinaigrette, or on a cheese board. A coarse mustard also works stirred into pan sauces and potato salad, where it adds body as well as flavor.
