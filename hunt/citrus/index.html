Straight Answers
Specialty Citrus FAQ
What makes a Meyer lemon different from a regular lemon?

A Meyer is a cross between a lemon and a mandarin, so it is rounder, thinner-skinned, deep golden, and far less sharp — sweet enough to eat off the tree. The skin carries a floral, almost herbal aromatic oil that transforms zest, curds, and dressings. It bruises and spoils faster than a thick-skinned grocery lemon, which is exactly why the real thing is shipped direct in season rather than trucked year-round.

When is specialty citrus in season?

Most citrus peaks from late fall through early spring. Meyer lemons run roughly November to March, with many small growers opening pre-orders in February for a tight late-winter harvest. Blood oranges land in winter; bergamot and Buddha's hand are also cool-season. Because these growers ship tree-ripened in narrow windows rather than from cold storage, ordering when the season is on is the only way to get the fruit at its peak.

How should I store citrus and how long does it last?

Tree-ripened, unwaxed citrus is more perishable than the gassed supermarket kind, so keep it in the refrigerator's crisper, where it holds one to two weeks. Bring a piece to room temperature before juicing for more yield. If you have a glut, zest and juice it and freeze both — frozen zest and ice-cube trays of juice keep that fresh-picked aroma for months.

Why is unwaxed, tree-ripened citrus worth seeking out?

Supermarket citrus is often picked green, gassed to color, waxed, and stored for weeks or months, which flattens the flavor and coats the peel you might want to zest. Fruit picked ripe and shipped within days from a small grove still smells like the tree, and the unwaxed skin means the zest is clean to use. For anything where the peel matters — zest, candied peel, cocktails — it is a different ingredient entirely.
