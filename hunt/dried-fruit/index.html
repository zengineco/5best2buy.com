5 Dried Fruit Makers Worth the Hunt

Most supermarket dried fruit is treated with sulfur dioxide to keep it bright, and a lot of it is sweetened with added sugar it never needed. Real dried fruit — sun-dried, unsulphured, no sugar added — tastes like a concentrated version of the fresh fruit, and it comes from small orchards growing varieties bred for flavor instead of shipping. The makers below dry their own harvest, and a couple grow fruit you genuinely can't get any other way. Here's where to buy it.

Straight Answers
Dried Fruit FAQ
What does unsulphured mean, and why does it matter?

Sulphured fruit is treated with sulphur dioxide, which preserves the bright color — that neon-orange dried apricot — and extends shelf life. Unsulphured fruit skips it, so it dries to a deeper brown and tastes more like concentrated fresh fruit. It matters because sulphites trigger reactions in sensitive people, especially some asthmatics, and because the natural version has a fuller, less sharp flavor. The color you see is the honest tell.

What does "no added sugar" really mean on dried fruit?

Dried fruit is naturally high in sugar because drying concentrates the fruit's own fructose, so it will always test sweet. "No added sugar" means the maker did not sweeten it further, which many commodity brands do to plump the fruit and mask lesser varieties. With good fruit dried properly, added sugar is unnecessary — the concentrated flavor of a ripe Blenheim apricot or a wild fig carries itself.

Why are some dried apricots so much more expensive?

Most supermarket dried apricots are large Turkish varieties bred to be pretty and ship well. Prized varieties like the California Blenheim are smaller, more delicate, and grown by only a handful of farms, so they cost more and taste dramatically more complex and tart-sweet. You are paying for a nearly-lost variety and single-farm drying, not just fruit — which is exactly why the flavor is a different thing entirely.

How should I store dried fruit, and how long does it keep?

Keep it in an airtight container away from heat and light; a cool pantry is fine for a few months, and the refrigerator or freezer extends it to a year or more. Unsulphured fruit, having no preservative, is a little more perishable and may darken further over time, which is harmless. If it dries out too much, a brief steam or a day in a sealed bag with a slice of apple softens it back up.
