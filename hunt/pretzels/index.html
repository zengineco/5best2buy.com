5 Artisan Pretzel Makers Worth the Hunt

The pretzel is one of America's great regional crafts, and the mass bags do it dirty — uniform, salty, forgettable. The makers below hand-roll theirs the old way: Pennsylvania-Dutch sourdough hard pretzels twisted by hand and baked in decades-old ovens, or gourmet soft pretzels shipped frozen to finish at home. A few of these bakeries have been doing it the same way since the 1930s. Here's where to buy a pretzel with an actual pedigree.

Straight Answers
Artisan Pretzel FAQ
What is the difference between a hard sourdough pretzel and a soft pretzel?

A hard pretzel is a crunchy, shelf-stable snack, and the Pennsylvania-Dutch tradition makes it from sourdough, hand-rolled and baked dry so it keeps for weeks with a deep, tangy flavor. A soft pretzel is a fresh bread product, chewy and warm, meant to be eaten soon after baking, which is why makers like Eastern Standard and Milwaukee Pretzel Co. ship theirs frozen to finish at home. Different foods entirely that happen to share a shape.

What makes a hand-rolled pretzel better than a mass-market bag?

Hand-rolling and hand-twisting give each pretzel a slightly irregular, dense structure that a machine extrusion cannot fake, and traditional makers use real sourdough and old hearth ovens rather than a fast line. Hammond's has twisted its pretzels by hand since 1931; Revonah bakes in a decades-old brick hearth over a multi-day process. That method is why they taste toasted and tangy instead of uniformly salty and flat.

How do you store hard pretzels and reheat soft ones?

Hard sourdough pretzels keep for weeks in an airtight container away from humidity, which softens them; if they lose their snap, a few minutes in a low oven restores it. Soft pretzels are best kept frozen and baked or warmed straight from the freezer, exactly as the makers ship them, so they come out hot and chewy. Do not leave a soft pretzel at room temperature for long, since it stales within a day.

Are there pretzels without the surface salt?

Yes. Several traditional makers offer an unsalted version for people who want the sourdough flavor and crunch without the crystals; Martin's, for instance, bakes a no-salt pretzel alongside its salted one. This is not the same as low-sodium, since the dough itself is still seasoned, but it removes the coarse salt on the surface. It is a good option if the salted crunch is too much or you are serving them with a salty dip.
