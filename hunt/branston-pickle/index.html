Ploughman's & Brown Pickle FAQ
What is ploughman's or brown pickle?

It's a sweet, malty, chunky vegetable pickle — diced swede, carrot, onion, and cauliflower in a dark spiced sauce — built to eat with sharp cheddar, cold ham, and crusty bread in the classic British 'ploughman's lunch.' Branston is the household name, but it's a mass-market product; the traditional style was a small-maker craft.

Is Branston independent?

No. Branston Pickle is owned by Mizkan, a large Japanese food conglomerate that also holds Sarson's and Haywards. It's a fine product, but it's a factory brand — which is why we point you to Tracklements, a Wiltshire family firm still making the pickle by hand, as the independent pick.

How do I use brown pickle?

The canonical move is a ploughman's: a wedge of aged cheddar, thick bread and butter, pickled onions, and a generous spoon of brown pickle. It's also unbeatable in a cheese-and-pickle sandwich, on a cheese toastie, or alongside a pork pie. The sweetness cuts sharp cheese and rich cured meat.

Why is this shelf so thin on US makers?

The specific Branston-style sweet-brown chunky pickle is a British product American artisans mostly don't make — US preservers lean toward fruit chutneys and relishes instead. Rather than pad the shelf with things that aren't the real thing, we list the one verified independent maker's version (Tracklements) and the honest import routes, and we'd love a US maker to fill this gap.
