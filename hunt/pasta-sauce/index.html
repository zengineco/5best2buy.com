Straight Answers
Pasta Sauce FAQ
What's the difference between marinara and other jarred red sauces?

Marinara is the simplest — tomatoes, garlic, olive oil, and herbs, cooked quickly to stay bright. A "gravy" or Sunday sauce is simmered much longer, often with onions and sometimes meat, so it turns deeper and sweeter. Vodka and arrabbiata sauces start from a similar tomato base but add cream or chili respectively. The jars here mostly stick to the honest, short-ingredient marinara and slow-cooked-gravy end.

Why do good jarred sauces cost more than the mass brands?

The cheap jars stretch flavor with water, tomato paste, and added sugar, and use commodity tomatoes. A small maker using whole San Marzano or vine-ripened Italian tomatoes, real olive oil, and a short cook is buying better ingredients and making less of it. The San Marzano name in particular signals a specific variety grown in volcanic soil near Naples, prized for low acidity and rich flavor.

Why do some sauces add sugar and others refuse to?

Sugar is often added to mask the tinny, acidic taste of underripe or low-quality tomatoes. When a maker starts with genuinely ripe, sweet tomatoes, that correction is unnecessary, which is why the best small-batch sauces advertise no added sugar. If a homemade sauce ever tastes too sharp, cooks traditionally balance it with a grated carrot or a pat of butter rather than spoonfuls of sugar.

How long does an opened jar of pasta sauce last?

Refrigerate after opening and use it within about five to seven days, since these clean-label sauces have few or no preservatives. Keep it in a sealed container and always spoon with something clean. If you cannot finish it, jarred tomato sauce freezes very well for a few months — freeze it in portions so you can pull out exactly what one dinner needs.
