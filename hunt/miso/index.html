5 Craft Miso Makers Worth the Hunt

Most supermarket miso is pasteurized, mass-produced, and flat — a shadow of what miso can be. Real craft miso is alive: soybeans and rice koji and salt, fermented for months or years in wooden vats, often unpasteurized so the culture stays active. A spoonful transforms a soup, a glaze, a dressing. The makers below are small American fermenters doing it the traditional way. One note: live miso often ships in cooler months only, so check the season. Here's where to buy it.

Straight Answers
Miso FAQ
What's the difference between white, yellow, and red miso?

The color tracks how long the miso is aged and how much soybean it contains. White (shiro) miso is fermented briefly and is mild, sweet, and mellow — good for dressings and light soups. Yellow (shinshu) is aged a bit longer and more balanced and all-purpose. Red (aka) miso is aged for a year or more, so it turns dark, deeply savory, and salty — best for hearty soups, braises, and glazes.

What does unpasteurized or "live" miso mean, and does it matter?

Traditional miso is fermented with live koji cultures, and unpasteurized miso keeps those cultures active, which is why it must be shipped and stored cold and often only ships in cooler months. The live enzymes give it more depth and probiotic benefit, but they also mean you should add it off the heat — boiling miso kills the cultures and dulls the flavor. Stir it into soup at the very end.

How do you store miso, and does it expire?

Keep miso in the refrigerator in a sealed container, and it lasts a very long time — often a year or more — because the high salt content preserves it. It rarely spoils in an unsafe way; instead it slowly darkens and deepens in flavor as it continues to age. A thin layer of liquid (tamari) or harmless surface changes can be stirred back in, and pressing plastic wrap against the surface helps keep it fresh.

What do you actually make with miso besides soup?

Miso is a savory backbone far beyond soup: whisk it into salad dressings and marinades, glaze fish or eggplant with it, stir a spoonful into butter for corn or vegetables, or use it to deepen braises, ramen, and even caramel. Because it is intensely salty and savory, a little replaces or supplements salt while adding umami. The lighter white styles are the easiest to start experimenting with.
