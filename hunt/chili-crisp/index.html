5 Small-Batch Chili Crisps Worth the Hunt

Chili crisp went from secret weapon to grocery staple fast, and most of the new shelf versions are forgettable. The small makers below are the ones actually worth keeping in the fridge — distinct recipes, real ingredients, and the kind of crunch and heat you end up putting on everything. A couple are genuinely hard to find. Here's where to get them.

Published June 2026 · Updated 29 Jun 2026

How this list works. Every maker here is small or independent, uses real ingredients, and earns its spot on merit — nobody pays to be listed. One note for the strict eaters: most of these are vegan, but where a jar contains fish or shellfish we say so plainly, and point you to the vegan version when there is one.
No.1 · The Easy First Jar

Red Clay Southern Chili Crisp

Charleston, SC

Red Clay's Southern Chili Crisp is a peanut-oil-based take with Fresno chili and shallot — a clean, crunchy, Southern spin on the format that's become a cult favorite for good reason. It's the most widely available of this list and the easiest to recommend as a first jar, from a genuinely small Charleston operation. Put it on a fried egg once and you'll get it.

Why it isn't on AmazonA small Southern maker built on one clean, specific recipe is a different thing than a commodity jar. The recipe and the smallness are the whole point.

See it at Red Clay →
No.2 · The Garlic-Forward One

KariKari

Seattle, WA · chef-made, small batch

KariKari is a Seattle maker doing a chef-made garlic chili crisp in small batches, with a transparent ingredient list and a devoted following. It's the kind of jar that runs out faster than you expect because you start putting it on everything. A great pick if garlic-forward crunch is what you're after.

Why it isn't on AmazonChef-made, small-batch crisp with a short, honest ingredient list is a hands-on operation, made in quantities a warehouse model wouldn't bother with.

See it at KariKari →
No.3 · The Deep-Savory Standout

Boon Sauce

Los Angeles, CA · slow-cooked chili oil

Boon is a slow-cooked LA chili oil deepened with anchovy for a savory, umami undertow — distinctive enough that it has a real connoisseur following. Worth knowing up front: it contains anchovy, so it isn't vegan (the anchovy is a sustainable, non-endangered fish). If you love the deep-savory end of chili oil, this one's a standout.

Why it isn't on AmazonA slow-cooked, anchovy-deepened chili oil made in limited batches is a chef's project, not a mass product. The distinctiveness is exactly why it's worth tracking down.

See it at Boon →
No.4 · The Brighter, Different One

Homiah Sambal Chili Crunch

New York, NY · Nyonya/Malaysian style

Homiah makes a low-oil Sambal Chili Crunch in the Nyonya/Malaysian style — women- and AAPI-founded, no preservatives, gluten-free, with a vegan Original that's the one to start with. It's a genuinely different flavor profile than the Sichuan-style crisps, brighter and more aromatic. A good pick if you want out of the usual chili-crisp lane.

Why it isn't on AmazonA small, founder-led maker doing a regional style in small batches is exactly the kind of producer the big platforms bury under the generic stuff.

See it at Homiah →
No.5 · The Deep Cut

Su Chili Crisp

Taipei recipe, via Yun Hai · no added sugar

Su Chili Crisp is a three-generation Taipei family recipe with no added sugar, sold through Yun Hai, a small shop that curates hard-to-find Taiwanese pantry goods. This is the deepest cut here — the one most people have never heard of and can't easily find — which is exactly why it belongs on the list. Yun Hai itself is worth a browse if you like discovering this kind of thing.

Why it isn't on AmazonA three-generation family recipe sold through a small curated pantry shop is the definition of hard-to-find. You get there because someone points you to it.

See it at Yun Hai →
