Fresh Cranberries Worth the Hunt

Almost all American cranberries funnel through one giant cooperative, which is why the fresh berries and the canned jelly all taste more or less the same. But a handful of small, independent bogs still sell direct — hand-sorted fresh berries and dried cranberries harvested at peak, from families who've farmed the same wetlands for generations. For a real cranberry sauce (which takes about ten minutes and ruins you for the can), start with a real cranberry. Fresh berries ship October through Thanksgiving.

Straight Answers
Fresh Cranberries FAQ
When are fresh cranberries in season?

The cranberry harvest runs from roughly late September through November, which is why fresh berries appear in stores only in the fall and disappear after the holidays. Small bogs ship fresh from about October through Thanksgiving, so that window is when to order. Outside it, your options are frozen (which works just as well for sauce) or dried.

What is the difference between wet-harvested and dry-harvested cranberries?

Wet harvesting floods the bog and knocks the berries loose to float — it is efficient and produces most of the fruit destined for juice and canning, but the berries bruise more and do not keep as long. Dry harvesting rakes the berries off the vine without flooding, yielding firmer fruit that stores better, which is why dry-harvested berries are the ones sold fresh. It is slower and more expensive, so it is a mark of a careful grower.

Can I freeze fresh cranberries, and do I thaw them first?

Yes — cranberries freeze exceptionally well, which is why they are one of the easiest fruits to stock up on. Just toss the unwashed bag straight into the freezer, where they keep for up to a year, and rinse them when you use them. For sauce or baking, add them frozen with no need to thaw; thawed berries turn soft and are harder to work with.

How do I make real cranberry sauce from fresh berries?

Simmer a bag of fresh or frozen cranberries with sugar and a splash of water or orange juice for about ten minutes, until the berries pop and the sauce thickens on its own — cranberries are so high in natural pectin that they set without anything added. Sweeten to taste, since the berries are genuinely tart, and cool it before serving. It keeps in the fridge for over a week and ruins you for the canned jelly.
