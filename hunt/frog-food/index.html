Frog & Amphibian Food FAQ
What do frogs eat?

Most pet frogs and amphibians are insectivores that eat live, moving prey — crickets, flightless fruit flies, worms, and dubia roaches — gut-loaded and dusted with calcium and vitamins. Fully aquatic frogs like African clawed and dwarf frogs also take sinking pellets and frozen foods such as bloodworms.

Do frogs eat live food only?

Most terrestrial frogs and toads need live, moving prey to trigger feeding. Aquatic frogs (African clawed and dwarf frogs) will take pellets and frozen foods. Dart frogs need very small live prey — flightless fruit flies and springtails — because of their tiny mouths.

What is gut-loading and dusting?

Gut-loading means feeding your feeder insects a nutritious diet for 24–48 hours before offering them, so the frog gets that nutrition. Dusting means coating the insects with a calcium and vitamin (with D3) powder right before feeding. Both are essential to prevent metabolic bone disease in amphibians.

What do dart frogs eat?

Dart frogs eat tiny prey — flightless (wingless) fruit flies of the Melanogaster and Hydei species, plus springtails and bean beetles. Because they eat so many small insects, dusting with a quality amphibian calcium/vitamin supplement at nearly every feeding is critical.
