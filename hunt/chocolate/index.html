5 Bean-to-Bar Chocolate Makers Worth the Hunt

Most chocolate hides where its cacao comes from for a reason — the supply chain has real problems. Bean-to-bar makers do the opposite: they buy the beans themselves, often direct from named farms, and turn them into bars in small batches. It tastes like actual chocolate instead of sugar, and you can find out who grew it. The makers below are among the most transparent in the country. Here's where to buy them.

Published June 2026 · Updated 29 Jun 2026

How this list works. Every maker here is small, controls its own bean-to-bar process, and earns its spot on merit — nobody pays to be listed. In chocolate, ethics means cacao sourcing, so that's weighted heavily here: direct trade, named origins, farmers paid well above market. We point you to the people doing it honestly.
No.1 · The Certified Standard

Taza Chocolate

Somerville, MA · stone-ground

Taza makes stone-ground, Mexican-style chocolate with a gritty, bright texture unlike anything in a candy aisle — and they built the industry's first third-party-certified Direct Trade cacao program, audited every year, with public transparency reports and farmers paid well above market. It's the rare case where the ethics are genuinely verified rather than claimed. Their rustic discs are the place to start.

Why it isn't on AmazonA certified, audited direct-trade program and a distinctive stone-ground process are deliberate, company-defining choices — not something a commodity bar bothers with.

No.2 · The Transparency Pioneer

Askinosie Chocolate

Springfield, MO · single-origin, profit-share

Askinosie is one of the original American direct-trade makers — single-origin bars, an open-book approach, and an actual profit-share with the farmers who grow the cacao. It's deeply story-driven without being precious about it, and the chocolate is excellent. If you want a maker whose ethics are baked into the business model rather than the marketing, this is it.

Why it isn't on AmazonProfit-sharing with farmers and publishing the numbers is a whole way of running a company. That doesn't translate to a marketplace listing.

See it at Askinosie →
No.3 · The Two-Ingredient Purist

Dandelion Chocolate

San Francisco, CA · single-origin bean-to-bar

Dandelion is famous for making bars from just two ingredients — cacao and sugar — so the flavor of each single origin comes through completely. They're serious about origin relationships and transparency, and tasting their bars side by side is the clearest lesson there is in how much the farm matters. A great pick if you want to actually taste the difference between origins.

Why it isn't on AmazonTwo-ingredient, single-origin bars depend entirely on bean quality and sourcing relationships — the kind of thing that gets lost the moment it's a generic listing.

See it at Dandelion →
No.4 · The Unroasted Original

Raaka Chocolate

Brooklyn, NY · unroasted, organic

Raaka makes chocolate from unroasted — "virgin" — cacao, which keeps the bright, fruity flavors that roasting usually mutes, and the result tastes genuinely different from any other bar you've had. It's organic, transparently sourced, and adventurous with flavors. The most distinctive pick on the list, and a fun one if you think you already know what chocolate tastes like.

Why it isn't on AmazonBuilding a brand on unroasted cacao and transparent organic sourcing is a deliberate, small-batch craft. The distinctiveness is the point, and scale would erase it.

See it at Raaka →
No.5 · The Craft-Scene Favorite

Dick Taylor Craft Chocolate

Eureka, CA · single-origin small batch

Dick Taylor makes small-batch, single-origin bars that are beloved among people deep in craft chocolate — beautifully made, quietly excellent, the kind of bar you give someone to show them what chocolate can be. It's the most classic "you'd never find this on your own" pick here. Start with whatever single origin sounds good and go from there.

Why it isn't on AmazonA tiny maker producing single-origin bars in small runs exists in limited quantities by nature. You find Dick Taylor through people who care about chocolate, not through an algorithm.
