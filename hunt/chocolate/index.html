5 Bean-to-Bar Chocolate Makers Worth the Hunt

Most chocolate hides where its cacao comes from for a reason — the supply chain has real problems. Bean-to-bar makers do the opposite: they buy the beans themselves, often direct from named farms, and turn them into bars in small batches. It tastes like actual chocolate instead of sugar, and you can find out who grew it. The makers below are among the most transparent in the country. Here's where to buy them.

Published June 2026 · Updated 29 Jun 2026

How this list works. Every maker here is small, controls its own bean-to-bar process, and earns its spot on merit — nobody pays to be listed. In chocolate, ethics means cacao sourcing, so that's weighted heavily here: direct trade, named origins, farmers paid well above market. We point you to the people doing it honestly.

Taza Chocolate

Somerville, MA · stone-ground

Taza makes stone-ground, Mexican-style chocolate with a gritty, bright texture unlike anything in a candy aisle — and they built the industry's first third-party-certified Direct Trade cacao program, audited every year, with public transparency reports and farmers paid well above market. It's the rare case where the ethics are genuinely verified rather than claimed. Their rustic discs are the place to start.

Springfield, MO · single-origin, profit-share

It's deeply story-driven without being precious about it, and the chocolate is excellent. If you want a maker whose ethics are baked into the business model rather than the marketing, this is it.

San Francisco, CA · single-origin bean-to-bar

Dandelion is famous for making bars from just two ingredients — cacao and sugar — so the flavor of each single origin comes through completely. They're serious about origin relationships and transparency, and tasting their bars side by side is the clearest lesson there is in how much the farm matters. A great pick if you want to actually taste the difference between origins.

Straight Answers
Bean-to-Bar Chocolate FAQ
What does bean-to-bar actually mean?

It means the same maker controls every step from the raw cacao bean to the finished bar — sourcing the beans, roasting, cracking, grinding, and conching in-house rather than buying pre-made chocolate and just re-melting it. Most supermarket brands melt down industrial couverture; a bean-to-bar maker can tell you the farm the beans came from. That control is what lets flavor and sourcing ethics be real rather than marketing.

What do the cacao percentages on the label mean?

The percentage is how much of the bar comes from the cacao bean — cocoa solids plus cocoa butter — with the rest mostly sugar. A 70% dark bar is 70% cacao and about 30% sugar; a 100% bar is pure cacao with no sugar at all. Higher percentage means less sweet and more intense, but it is not automatically better — a well-made 70% single-origin can taste far more complex than a harsh 85%.

What is the difference between single-origin and blended chocolate?

Single-origin means the cacao comes from one country, region, or even one farm, so you taste the distinct character of that place — bright and fruity, earthy, nutty. Blended chocolate mixes beans from many sources for a consistent house flavor year-round. Single-origin bars are the clearest way to taste how much the farm and the bean genetics matter, which is why tasting several side by side is so revealing.

Why does craft dark chocolate taste so different from a grocery bar?

Real bean-to-bar dark chocolate is usually just cacao and sugar, sometimes with a little added cocoa butter, so the natural fruit and roast notes of the bean come through. Mass-market bars lean on heavy sugar, vanilla, extra milk solids, and sometimes vegetable fats that flatten everything into one generic sweet taste. The craft version can taste of cherry, citrus, or coffee because nothing was added to cover the bean up.
