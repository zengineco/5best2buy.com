Straight Answers
Matcha FAQ
What is the difference between ceremonial and culinary grade?

Ceremonial grade is made from the youngest first-harvest leaves, shade-grown and stone-ground fine, and it is meant to be whisked with just water — vivid green, naturally sweet, smooth. Culinary grade uses later, coarser leaves and is built to hold its flavor against milk and sugar in lattes and baking, so it tastes stronger and more bitter on its own. Neither is fake; they are made for different jobs. If you are drinking it straight, you want ceremonial.

How do I actually whisk it so it is not clumpy?

Sift about a teaspoon of matcha through a small strainer to break up clumps, add a couple ounces of water at roughly 175F — not boiling, which scorches it and turns it bitter — and whisk. Use a bamboo whisk (chasen) in a brisk W or M motion, not a circular stir, until a fine foam forms on top. A small electric frother works in a pinch. Boiling water and a slow stir are the two most common mistakes.

How much caffeine is in matcha compared to coffee?

Because you drink the whole ground leaf rather than an infusion, a serving of matcha carries a fair amount of caffeine — generally less than a full cup of brewed coffee, but more than steeped green tea. It also contains L-theanine, an amino acid that tends to make the lift feel steadier and less jittery than coffee. The exact amount varies by how much powder you use.

Why does matcha go stale so fast, and how do I store it?

Once leaves are ground into powder, the huge surface area makes matcha oxidize quickly — it dulls from bright green to yellow-brown and loses its sweetness within weeks of opening. Keep the tin sealed airtight in the fridge or freezer, away from light and air, and let it come to room temperature before opening to avoid condensation. Buy small amounts and drink them within a month or two of opening. Vivid color is the quickest freshness tell.
