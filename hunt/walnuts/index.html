The growers below sell this year's harvest direct: single orchards, regenerative farms, rare red-skinned varietals, and one native black walnut foraged from the wild. Buy close to the harvest and you'll never go back.

Straight Answers
Walnuts FAQ
Why do walnuts go rancid so fast, and how do I store them?

Walnuts are among the highest in fragile polyunsaturated oils of any nut, and those oils oxidize quickly when exposed to air, heat, and light — that oxidation is the bitter, paint-like taste of an old walnut. Keep shelled walnuts in an airtight container in the fridge for a few months, or the freezer for a year or more; the freezer is genuinely the best home for them. In-shell walnuts last longer because the shell protects the oils. Buy what you will use in a season rather than stockpiling at room temperature.

What is the difference between black walnuts and English walnuts?

The mild, tan walnut in every supermarket is the English (Persian) walnut, easy to crack and gentle in flavor. The American black walnut is a different native species — intensely bold, almost smoky, with a hard shell that is notoriously difficult to crack, which is why it never became a mass commodity. Black walnuts stand up in baking where English walnuts would disappear, giving a darker, more assertive result. They are a distinct ingredient, not a substitute.

What does fresh-crop actually mean, and why does it matter?

Fresh-crop means this season's harvest rather than nuts pooled and warehoused from prior years, which is common in the commodity supply. Because walnut oils turn so fast, a current-crop nut bought close to harvest is sweet and buttery, while an aged one drifts toward bitter before you ever open the bag. Single-orchard growers can tell you the crop year because they grew it. Fall through winter is when the freshest walnuts ship.

Are raw or unpasteurized walnuts different, and are they safe?

Most walnuts sold in the US are lightly pasteurized by steam or treatment for safety, and raw here usually means unroasted rather than truly untreated. Genuinely unpasteurized walnuts, sold by some small farms, keep more of their living, delicate flavor and the intact oils. They are generally safe from a careful small grower, though as with any raw food the extra-cautious may prefer pasteurized. Never-heated nuts in particular keep the sweetness that heat can flatten.
