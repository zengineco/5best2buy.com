5 Small-Roaster Coffees Worth the Hunt

The coffee in a grocery bag was usually roasted months ago by a company that buys on price. Small roasters work the other way: small batches, shipped within days, and most can tell you which farm or co-op the beans came from. A couple of these lead with ethics, one or two with sheer freshness, and all of them are a real step up from the shelf. If you've got a grinder, start with whole bean.

Straight Answers
Coffee FAQ
Whole bean or ground — does it actually matter?

Coffee starts losing aroma within minutes of grinding, because grinding exposes far more surface area to air. If you own any grinder, buy whole bean and grind right before you brew — it is the single biggest jump in cup quality most people can make. Pre-ground is fine if it is the only way you will drink better coffee, but a fresh bag ground at the roaster is already fading by the time it reaches you.

What does the roast date tell me, and how long is the coffee good?

Roast date is the number that matters — not a printed best-by, which the commodity brands use because their coffee is already old. Beans are at their best from about five days to four weeks after roasting; the first few days they are still releasing gas and can taste sharp. Small roasters print or stamp the roast date because roasting to order is the whole point. Buy what you will drink in a month.

Light, medium, or dark roast?

Darker roasting burns off the origin character and leaves a heavier, more uniform roast flavor, which is why big brands lean dark — it hides mediocre beans. Light and medium roasts keep the fruit, acidity, and single-origin traits the farm actually grew. If you want to taste where the coffee came from, start medium and go lighter; if you want bold and smoky, go dark. Neither is stronger in caffeine to any meaningful degree.

How should I store beans — is the freezer a good idea?

Keep beans in an airtight, opaque container at room temperature, away from heat, light, and moisture. Do not keep them in the fridge, where they pick up odors and condensation every time you open the door. Long-term, a sealed freezer bag works if you portion it and never refreeze a thawed bag — but the better fix is simply buying fresh, smaller amounts more often.
