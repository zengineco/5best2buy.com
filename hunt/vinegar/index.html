5 Artisan Vinegar Makers Worth the Hunt

Most supermarket vinegar is industrial acetic acid, made fast and standardized to taste like nothing in particular. Real vinegar is a slow ferment — wine or cider turned by live cultures over months, often aged in barrels — and it has the kind of depth that makes a salad or a sauce suddenly work. The makers below do it the old way, and a few are good enough that Michelin kitchens cook with them. Here's where to buy it.

Straight Answers
Vinegar FAQ
Does vinegar ever go bad?

Not really — vinegar's acidity makes it self-preserving, so it stays safe essentially indefinitely. What you may see over time is harmless: a cloudy haze, some sediment, or a gelatinous blob called a "mother" forming in raw, unfiltered vinegar. None of that is spoilage. Just keep it capped and out of direct sunlight and it will keep for years.

What do the grades on balsamic vinegar actually mean?

True traditional balsamic (Aceto Balsamico Tradizionale DOP) is aged in wood for a minimum of 12 years and is thick, syrupy, and expensive — a finishing drizzle, not a salad base. "Balsamic Vinegar of Modena IGP" is the far more common everyday grade, made faster with added wine vinegar. Most supermarket balsamic is younger still, sometimes just colored with caramel, which is why a real barrel-aged bottle tastes like a different product.

Wine, cider, or rice vinegar — when do I use each?

Wine vinegar (red or white) is sharp and clean, the workhorse for vinaigrettes and pan sauces. Cider vinegar is fruitier and rounder, good for slaws, braises, and anything Southern. Rice vinegar is mild and slightly sweet, built for sushi rice, dressings, and East Asian cooking. Matching the vinegar to the dish matters as much as the quality of the bottle.

What is "the mother" and should I throw it out?

The mother is a natural colony of acetic-acid bacteria and cellulose that forms in raw, unpasteurized vinegar — the same living culture that turned the wine or cider into vinegar in the first place. It is completely harmless and a sign the vinegar is alive and unfiltered. You can strain it out, leave it, or even use it to start a batch of your own. It is not a defect.
