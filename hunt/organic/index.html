Straight Answers
Organic FAQ
What does USDA Certified Organic actually require?

USDA Organic is a legally enforced standard: crops are grown without synthetic pesticides or fertilizers, no GMOs, and no sewage-sludge or irradiation, on land free of prohibited substances for at least three years. Producers are inspected annually by an accredited certifier, and the supply chain has to be documented. It is meaningfully stricter and more auditable than an unregulated marketing word.

What is the difference between organic and natural?

This is the crux of the shelf. Organic is a defined, certified, third-party-audited standard, while natural has almost no legal meaning on most foods and any brand can print it. A product can be labeled natural and still contain conventionally grown, pesticide-treated ingredients. When it matters, look for the certification seal, not the vibe word.

What do the different organic labels mean?

The USDA has tiers. One hundred percent organic means every ingredient is certified organic; the organic seal covers products that are at least ninety-five percent organic ingredients; made with organic covers at least seventy percent and cannot use the main seal. Reading which tier a product claims tells you how much of it is genuinely certified rather than partially.

Is organic worth the higher price?

That depends on what you value. You are paying for the certified growing practices, the reduced synthetic-pesticide exposure, and often the environmental and small-farm sourcing behind it, not necessarily a big nutritional difference. For some foods, especially thin-skinned produce and things you eat a lot of, many buyers think the trade is worth it; for others it matters less. The point of this shelf is that when you do pay for organic, it is the real, certified thing.
