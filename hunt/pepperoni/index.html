Pepperoni Worth the Hunt

Not all pepperoni is created equal, and any pizza lover already knows it. The great stuff is stuffed in natural casing so the slices curl up into little crisp-edged cups that hold pools of rendered fat and paprika — "cup and char," the holy grail on a pie. Then there's old-world artisan pepperoni: dry-cured salumi with real fermented depth. The floppy, greasy commodity slice is a different food entirely. Below are the makers the best pizzaiolos actually reach for.

Straight Answers
Pepperoni FAQ
What is "cup and char" pepperoni and why does everyone want it?

Cup-and-char pepperoni is stuffed in a natural casing with a smaller diameter, so on a hot pizza the slices curl up at the edges into little crisp cups that catch pools of rendered fat and paprika oil. It's the opposite of the flat, floppy deli slice that lies limp on the pie. Pizzaiolos chase it because it delivers crisp edges, concentrated spice, and that signature charred rim in every bite.

What's the difference between pizza pepperoni and old-world artisan pepperoni?

Pizza pepperoni — the cup-and-char kind — is a semi-dry, cooked-and-smoked sausage built to perform under a broiler. Old-world artisan pepperoni is closer to true salumi: ground pork and spice fermented and slow dry-cured over weeks, developing a tangy, complex depth you eat as-is on a board. Both are "pepperoni," but one is engineered for the oven and the other for the slicer.

Why does natural casing matter for pepperoni?

Natural casing is the key to the cup: it shrinks and tightens as the pepperoni cooks, pulling the slice edges upward so they crisp and char. Collagen casings can be tuned to do something similar, which is why some small makers use them deliberately. Skinless, large-diameter commodity pepperoni has no casing to curl, so it just sits flat and renders grease across the pie.

How do I store pepperoni, and can I freeze it?

An unopened stick of dry-cured pepperoni is largely shelf-stable, but once cut or opened keep it wrapped and refrigerated, where it holds for a few weeks. Sliced pizza pepperoni freezes very well — portion it flat in a sealed bag and pull slices straight onto a pie. Whole sticks can be frozen too; thaw in the fridge, and expect a firmer, drier texture the longer a dry-cured stick sits, which is normal rather than spoilage.
