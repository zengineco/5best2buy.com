Straight Answers
Cat Food FAQ
Why is wet or fresh food better than dry kibble for cats?

Cats evolved from desert animals and have a weak thirst drive, so they are built to get most of their water from prey, not a bowl. Kibble is only about ten percent moisture, which leaves many cats chronically underhydrated and is linked to urinary and kidney problems. Wet, fresh, or raw food runs around seventy to eighty percent moisture, closer to what a cat's body expects, and it is also lower in the starchy carbohydrate that dry food needs to hold its shape.

What does obligate carnivore actually mean for feeding?

An obligate carnivore must get key nutrients from animal tissue and cannot make them from plants. Cats require taurine, preformed vitamin A, and arachidonic acid from meat, and they need a high-protein, low-carbohydrate diet to thrive. This is why a cat cannot be healthy on a dog food or a vegetarian diet, and why meat-first formulas matter more for cats than almost any other pet.

Is raw cat food safe?

Raw feeding carries a real bacterial risk (salmonella, listeria) for both the cat and the people handling it, so hygiene, a reputable maker, and careful storage matter. Makers reduce that risk with USDA-inspected ingredients, careful sourcing, and sometimes high-pressure processing, and gently-cooked fresh food is a lower-risk middle ground. Households with immune-compromised people or very young kids should weigh cooked options and talk to their vet.

How do I transition a cat to a new food?

Go slow, because cats can be stubborn and an abrupt switch causes stomach upset or a flat refusal to eat. Mix a little of the new food into the old and increase it over seven to ten days, or longer for a fussy cat. Never let an overweight cat go without eating for more than a day or two during a switch, as cats that stop eating can develop dangerous fatty liver disease.
