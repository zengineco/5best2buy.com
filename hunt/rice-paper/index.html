Rice Paper & Spring Roll Wrappers Worth the Hunt

Grocery-store rice paper is usually one stiff, cracks-when-you-fold-it brand hiding in the ethnic aisle. The good stuff is Vietnamese banh trang — thin tapioca-and-rice rounds that turn pliable in warm water and roll tight for fresh summer rolls, or crisp up for fried cha gio. These sellers stock the real range: 16cm to 22cm, plain, and the sesame-studded frying kind.

Rice Paper & Spring Roll Wrappers FAQ
How do I use rice paper without it tearing?

Dip one round in warm (not hot) water for just a few seconds — it should still feel slightly stiff when you lay it down, because it keeps softening on the counter. Overdip and it turns to glue. Fill the bottom third, fold the sides in, and roll tight like a burrito. Work one at a time until you get the timing.

What's the difference between the sizes?

Diameter is the main choice: 16cm rounds make small, one-bite appetizer rolls; 22cm rounds make full-size summer rolls or a rice-paper wrap you can load up. Some wrappers are made specifically for frying (often with sesame) and crisp better than the thin fresh-roll kind. Match the size to the roll you want.

Is rice paper the same as spring-roll or egg-roll wrappers?

No. Rice paper (banh trang) is a translucent tapioca-and-rice round you soften in water for fresh Vietnamese summer rolls or fry for cha gio. Spring-roll and egg-roll wrappers are wheat-flour sheets that are always fried or pan-cooked. If a recipe calls for fresh rolls you don't cook, you want rice paper.

How long does rice paper keep?

It's a shelf-stable dried product and keeps for a year or more in a cool, dry cupboard. Keep the pack sealed so the rounds don't absorb humidity and stick together or warp. If a few get brittle at the edges, the centers usually still roll fine.
