A Field Guide

The Traditional Nut Roll

Potica, povitica, beigli, makowiec, orehnjača, cozonac, gubana — one idea, carried down a dozen valleys and across an ocean: an enriched yeast dough worked thin, spread with ground walnut or poppy, and wound into a tight spiral.

Published 4 July 2026 · Sourced & fact-checked 4 July 2026

Nearly every culture from the Alps to the Carpathians makes a version of the same cake, and they are more closely related than their names let on. Strip away the borders and the recipe is always this: a rich, yeasted dough rolled or stretched thin, covered edge-to-edge with a dense nut or seed filling, and rolled up so each slice shows a spiral. The names even cluster around three old words — poviti, "to wrap"; guba, "to fold"; and the filling itself, orah (walnut) or mak (poppy). The name, in other words, is the technique.

Two things separate one from the next. The first is the dough: some are rolled out with a pin (the everyday versions), while the proudest — potica and povitica — are hand-stretched over a cloth until you can see light through them, the way a strudel is pulled. The second is the filling: almost every member of the family has a walnut twin and a poppy twin, sometimes under the same name. Everything else — the shape, the pan, the feast it belongs to — is a regional accent on one very old, very good idea.

This guide's heart lives on its sister site, poticas.com — where the story, the memory, and a walnut potica made the old way are kept. Worth The Hunt handles the other half: the map of the whole family, and where to buy the real ingredients to make one yourself.

The Family

One idea, a dozen valleys

Potica · Slovenia

poviti, "to wrap" · hand-stretched dough · walnut is the classic
Walnut · Poppy · TarragonChristmas · Easter · Weddings

The Slovenian festive cake, and the family's most refined member. The dough is stretched paper-thin like strudel, then rolled with a filling — orehova (walnut) is the classic, but Slovenes also make pehtranova (tarragon), makova (poppy), medena (honey), and more. It's baked in a round fluted ring pan with a central cone, the potičnik, which gives potica its signature crown shape.

Povitica · Croatia & Croatian-America

poviti, "to wrap" — the same root as potica · hand-stretched dough
WalnutStrawberry Hill, Kansas City

Potica's close cousin, sharing the exact same root word. The dough is stretched extremely thin over a cloth-covered table, spread with buttery ground English walnut, and rolled into many tight swirled layers — famously baked as an "S"-shaped loaf rather than the Slovenian ring. It became the signature of the Strawberry Hill Croatian neighborhood of Kansas City, Kansas, whose families settled the strawberry-covered hills after the 1903 flood. (The oft-quoted "thin enough to read a newspaper through" is the bakery's own line, not a measurement — but the dough really is remarkably thin.)

Orehnjača & Makovnjača · Croatia & Serbia

orah, "walnut" / mak, "poppy" · rolled-out dough · the walnut/poppy twins
Walnut or PoppyChristmas · Easter

The everyday walnut roll (orehnjača) and its poppy-seed twin (makovnjača) — a perfect illustration of the family's walnut-or-poppy split. The reliable difference from povitica is technique: these use dough rolled out with a pin rather than hand-stretched to translucency. In casual use the names blur — you'll see a walnut roll sold as "orehnjača (Croatian povitica)" — so treat it as a strong tendency, not a rigid rule.

Makowiec · Poland

mak, "poppy" · here poppy is the star, not walnut
PoppyChristmas Eve (Wigilia)

Poland's contribution flips the default: the star is a dense ground poppy-seed filling — butter, honey, and usually raisins, walnuts, and candied orange peel — rolled into a brioche-like spiral log and finished with a sugar glaze. It's a fixture of the Polish Christmas Eve Wigilia table. Its walnut twin exists too, as strucla orzechowa (from orzech, walnut).

Beigli / Bejgli · Hungary

from German Beugel, "to bend" · thin dough, dense short log
Walnut (diós) · Poppy (mákos)Christmas · Easter

Hungary's holiday classic, traditionally served as a pair: diós (walnut) and mákos (poppy). Thin dough is rolled tightly into a short, dense log, sliced into pinwheels. Its trademark cracked, marbled crust is deliberate and clever — the loaf is brushed with egg yolk and left to dry, then with egg white, and left again until fine cracks form before it ever hits the oven.

Cozonac / Kozunak · Romania & Bulgaria

a softer, enriched loaf — a looser member of the family
Walnut · Cocoa · Turkish delightChristmas · Easter · New Year

More an enriched brioche than a thin-dough roll, so it sits at the family's edge — but the spirit is the same. Romanian cozonac swirls a soft, eggy dough with ground walnut (often with cocoa), raisins, poppy, or Turkish delight. Bulgarian kozunak leans plainer and braided, frequently just raisins. The etymology is genuinely unsettled — one traced derivation runs through Greek kosona, "cocoon" — so we leave it an open question rather than pretend otherwise.

Gubana & Putizza · Friuli & Trieste, Italy

gubana ← guba, "fold" · putizza ← Slovenian potica · Italy's Slovene-border spirals
Walnut · Raisin · Pine nut · GrappaChristmas · Easter · Weddings

Just across the Slovenian border, Friuli makes its own spirals filled with walnuts, raisins, pine nuts, candied peel, and a splash of grappa. Gubana (from Cividale del Friuli) is a free-form coiled loaf; putizza (from Trieste) uses a softer, richer dough baked in a round pan — and its name descends directly from Slovenian potica, a rare well-documented link. Gubana is protected as an Italian PAT (traditional food product) and by a makers' consortium — not, despite common claims, an IGP.

Orechovník · Slovakia, Rusyn & Czech lands

závin, "to roll up" · the versions that became the American "nut roll"
Walnut (orech) · Poppy (mak)Christmas · Easter

The Slovak walnut roll (orechovník) and poppy roll (makovník) — also ořechový závin in Czech — are the versions that, more than any other, gave America its catch-all name. Rolled into a log, walnut or poppy inside, they crossed the ocean with mill and mine families and simply became "nut roll" (and, confusingly, "kolachi," which in northeastern Ohio means the rolled log but elsewhere means a filled cookie).

The wider circle

cousins and adjacents — same idea, further out

Ukraine's makivnyk (poppy roll) is a full member, baked for Christmas and Velykden. The German-Austrian Nussstriezel / Nusszopf is adjacent — genuinely a braid rather than a closed log, so a cousin more than a sibling. And the poppy-roll name echoes on and on: Czech makový závin, Austrian Mohnstrudel, Latvian magonmaizīte, Lithuanian aguonų vyniotinis.

The American Story

The Nut Roll Belt

These didn't stay separate in America. The Central and Eastern European wave of roughly 1880–1920 — drawn by steel, coal, and mining — poured these communities into the same mill towns, where a dozen grandmothers' rolls flattened into one English name. The dense core of the belt is the steel corridor of Pittsburgh–Youngstown–Cleveland, with satellites strung along the mining and packing towns:

  • Slovenians → Minnesota's Iron Range, Cleveland, Colorado mining towns — potica. (Cleveland held the largest Slovene population outside Slovenia; in 1914 it was reckoned the third-most-populous Slovene city on earth, after Trieste and Ljubljana.)
  • Croatians → Kansas City's Strawberry Hill; also Pittsburgh and Cleveland — povitica.
  • Slovaks & Carpatho-Rusyns → Pittsburgh and western Pennsylvania, northeastern Ohio, the Youngstown valley — orechovník, "nut roll," "kolachi."
  • Poles → Chicago, the industrial Midwest, Pennsylvania — makowiec.
  • Hungarians → the same steel and mill regions — beigli.

The tidy one-community-per-city map is an honest approximation, not a hard rule — these groups overlapped in the same towns. What's solid is the presence: the nut roll is documented across Pittsburgh, Cleveland, Youngstown, Kansas City, the Iron Range, Butte, Joliet, and Sheboygan.

Setting the Record Straight

A short, honest history
The name is the technique
Potica is a contraction of the older povitica, from the verb poviti — "to wrap, to roll up." The cake is named for what your hands do to it.
First written, c. 1575 — but not in a cookbook
The earliest known mention of the word appears in the 16th-century writings of the Slovene Protestant reformer Primož Trubar (under the older form povitica) — a religious text, not a recipe book. That dates the word, not the dish's invention.
First described, 1689
The first detailed description comes from polymath Janez Vajkard Valvasor in The Glory of the Duchy of Carniola (Nuremberg, 1689), where potica appears among festive pastries as a thin dough filled with walnuts and honey.
Protected as "Slovenska potica," 2021
The name Slovenska potica is a registered EU Traditional Speciality Guaranteed (TSG) — Regulation (EU) 2021/656, April 2021 (nationally protected since 2017). A TSG protects the traditional name and method, not a geographic region. Potica holds no UNESCO status — a common mix-up worth retiring.
A cake that gets around
In 2017, Pope Francis reportedly asked Melania Trump, "What do you give him to eat, potica?" — a small sign of how far one wrapped cake has traveled from the valleys of Carniola.
