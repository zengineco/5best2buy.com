Mole Poblano, and Where to Source It

Mole poblano is the deep end — twenty-some ingredients, each toasted or fried and ground into one sauce that's smoky, fruity, spiced, and barely sweet all at once, poured over turkey the way Puebla has done it for centuries. It's a whole afternoon, and it's a masterclass in sourcing: nearly every part of it is a pantry ingredient you want to buy well anyway. Here's the method, and where to buy all twenty things — most of which you may already have hunted down.

Ancho, mulato, and pasilla are the holy trinity, with a chipotle for smoke. Mulato is the one generalists skip — don't. This blend is the soul of the sauce.

A Note on Origins
Where mole comes from — honestly
The beloved story — that a nun at Puebla's Convento de Santa Rosa improvised mole from pantry scraps to honor a visiting archbishop — is a lovely legend, not documented history. (There's a competing tale of a friar, Fray Pascual, knocking a tray of spices into a pot.) What's actually documented is better: mole poblano is a genuine colonial fusion — pre-Hispanic foundations (chiles, turkey, tomatoes, and cacao ground on the metate) meeting Old-World arrivals (cinnamon, cloves, anise, sesame, almonds) carried in on the Manila Galleon. The word itself is Nahuatl, mōlli, "sauce." Tellingly, the first written mole recipes don't appear until more than a century after the convent legend is set — which is exactly why historians say its precise origin can't be pinned down. The fusion is the real story, and it's the better one.
Part Two
The Method
  1. Toast & soak the chilesStem and seed the ancho, mulato, pasilla, and chipotle. Toast them on a dry comal just until fragrant — never blackened — then soak in hot water until soft and pliable.
  2. Fry everything elseIn lard, fry each element on its own so it browns evenly: almonds, sesame, pepitas, raisins, tomato, tomatillo, and the tortilla or bread. Toast the canela, cloves, pepper, and anise. This layering of toasted flavors is the work.
  3. Blend to a pasteBlend the soaked chiles with all the fried nuts, seeds, fruit, tomato, spices, and thickener into a thick, smooth paste, loosening with a little broth as needed. Work in batches; push it smooth.
  4. Fry the pasteHeat lard in a heavy pot and fry the paste, stirring constantly, until it darkens and thickens and smells transformed. Frying the paste — not just simmering it — is what makes mole taste like mole.
  5. Melt in the chocolateLoosen with broth, melt in the Mexican chocolate, and season with salt. Simmer low and stir often for at least an hour, until it's glossy and deep. Then balance it — a little more chocolate for roundness, chile for heat, salt to sharpen.
  6. ServePoach the turkey or chicken, then bathe the pieces in the mole. Shower with toasted sesame seeds and serve with rice and warm tortillas. Mole is even better the next day.
