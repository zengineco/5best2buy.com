Yakisoba, and Where to Source It

The name says soba but yakisoba uses springy wheat noodles, and the goal is to get char on both the noodles and the cabbage without steaming everything into mush. Cook on the highest heat your pan can take and keep the food moving in short bursts so the sauce caramelizes on the noodles instead of pooling.

Straight Answers
Can you make Yakisoba in an air fryer? — Yes

Yes — this is exactly what an air fryer does well. Work in a single layer so the air can move, and shake or flip halfway for even crisping. It runs a touch faster than the oven, so check early.

Can you freeze Yakisoba? — Yes

Yes — it freezes well. Cool it fully, portion into airtight containers, and it keeps a few months; thaw in the fridge and reheat gently.

Can you make Yakisoba ahead of time? — Prep ahead

Best fried to order — the crunch fades if it sits. Do the breading or shaping ahead and cook it fresh when you serve.

Is Yakisoba gluten-free? — Not as written

Not as written — it contains gluten. Make it gluten-free: swap in tamari instead of soy sauce (check the other labels too).

Is Yakisoba vegan? — No

No — it contains meat, so it isn’t vegetarian or vegan.

Part Two
The Method
  1. Prep everythingSlice pork thin, cut vegetables, and loosen the noodles; stir-frying leaves no time to chop.
  2. Render the porkSear pork in a screaming-hot pan until the edges brown and fat renders, about 2 minutes.
  3. Add the vegetablesToss in onion, carrot, then cabbage and cook until just wilted but still crisp.
  4. Char the noodlesAdd noodles and let them sit 30 seconds against the pan before tossing, to catch some color.
  5. Sauce and finishPour in the tonkatsu sauce and soy, toss until it coats and reduces, then scatter scallion.
