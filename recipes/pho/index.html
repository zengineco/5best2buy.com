Phở, and Where to Source It

A great bowl of phở is nothing but broth, noodles, and a few slices of beef — which means the broth has to carry everything, and it's built over six patient hours from bones, charred aromatics, and a small sachet of whole spices. Get those three things right — clean bones, a real fish sauce, whole spices you toast yourself — and you'll make a broth clearer and deeper than most restaurants. Here's the method, and where to buy every part.

A Note on Origins
Where phở comes from — honestly
Phở is younger than it feels — it took its modern form around 1900–1910 in northern Vietnam (Nam Định and Hanoi), and a detailed 1907 survey of Vietnamese food doesn't even mention it, evidence of how new it was. French colonial rule genuinely mattered: pre-colonial Vietnam kept cattle for work, not meat, and French demand created the beef bones and scraps cooks turned into broth. But the famous claim that "phở" comes from French pot-au-feu is mostly folk etymology — linguists find the stronger derivation is Chinese: from ngưu nhục phấn (牛肉粉, "beef rice noodles"), the Cantonese dish, clipped through street-vendor cries into "phở." The honest picture is a fusion of three influences — Cantonese beef-noodle, a Vietnamese buffalo-and-vermicelli soup, and French beef — not a single inventor or a tidy French origin.

Part Two
The Method
  1. Parboil the bonesCover the bones with cold water, boil hard for 10 minutes, then dump the water and rinse the bones well. This one step is the difference between a cloudy broth and a crystal-clear one.
  2. Char the aromaticsHalve the onions and the ginger and char them directly over a flame or under the broiler until blackened and sweet-smelling, then rinse off the loose char. This smoky depth is essential.
  3. Toast the sachetToast the star anise, cinnamon, cloves, coriander, fennel, and black cardamom in a dry pan until fragrant, then bundle them in a sachet or tea bag so they perfume the broth without scattering.
  4. Simmer low and longCombine the cleaned bones, brisket, charred aromatics, and sachet with fresh water and simmer gently — never a hard boil — for 4 to 6 hours, skimming the surface often. Pull the brisket once tender. Season with fish sauce, salt, and a little rock sugar until it sings.
  5. Cook the noodlesJust before serving, soak and briefly blanch the flat rice noodles — seconds, not minutes — and divide them into deep bowls.
  6. AssembleTop the noodles with sliced brisket (and paper-thin raw eye of round if you like — the boiling broth cooks it). Ladle the screaming-hot broth over, and bring it to the table with a plate of Thai basil, bean sprouts, lime, and chiles for everyone to build their own bowl.

Straight Answers
Can you make Phở in an air fryer? — Yes, with an insert

Not on its own — an air fryer can’t hold a pool of liquid. You can simmer it in an oven-safe dish or a silicone insert made to fit the basket, but the broth won’t reduce or brown the way it does on the stove, and that insert comes out scorching, so use oven mitts. For real depth, a pot on the stove wins.

Can you freeze Phở? — Yes

Yes — it freezes well. Cool it fully, portion into airtight containers, and it keeps a few months; thaw in the fridge and reheat gently.

Can you make Phở ahead of time? — Yes

Yes — it holds well and often tastes better the next day. Make it ahead and reheat gently before serving.

Is Phở gluten-free? — Yes

Yes — nothing in this recipe as written contains gluten. As always, glance at the labels on any packaged items (broth, sauces, chocolate), since brands vary.

Is Phở vegan? — No

No — it contains meat, so it isn’t vegetarian or vegan.
