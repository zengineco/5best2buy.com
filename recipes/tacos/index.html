Tacos, and Where to Source Them

A taco is honest food: a warm corn tortilla, something cooked with care, salsa, onion, cilantro, lime. There's no sauce to hide behind and no long cook to forgive a shortcut — which means the ingredients are the whole game. Real nixtamalized masa instead of a cold supermarket tortilla, a salsa built from toasted dried chiles instead of a jar, cheese from an actual creamery — and a taco stops being Tuesday filler and becomes the thing you'd stand at a cart in the rain for. Here's the method, and exactly where to buy the parts that matter.

A Note on Origins
Where the taco comes from — honestly
The taco is often wrapped in ancient mystique, but the honest record is more recent and more interesting. Food historian Jeffrey Pilcher (Planet Taco) traces the earliest references to the taco as food only to the end of the 1800s — it is not a pre-Columbian dish. The most-repeated origin story — that "taco" first named the little paper-wrapped gunpowder charges miners used in 18th-century Mexican silver mines — is Pilcher's hypothesis, not settled fact; he says plainly that the taco's true origins are unknown. What's documented: tacos spread through working-class Mexican communities, appeared in U.S. newspapers by around 1905 alongside mining and railroad migration, and the hard-shell taco predates Taco Bell — Mexican cookbooks described frying tortillas into U-shapes back in the 1940s. We'd rather tell you what's known than sell you a legend.
Part Two
The Method
  1. Make the salsaToast the guajillo, ancho, and árbol chiles on a dry, hot comal for a few seconds per side — until fragrant, never blackened, which turns them bitter. Soak them in hot water 15–20 minutes, then blend with a little soaking liquid, a clove of garlic, salt, and a splash of vinegar or lime into a smooth, deep-red salsa.
  2. Cook the proteinFor carne asada, season the skirt steak with salt, cumin, and Mexican oregano and sear it hard on a screaming-hot cast-iron pan or grill, 2–3 minutes a side; rest it, then chop across the grain. For carnitas, simmer cubed pork shoulder low and slow in leaf lard with salt and aromatics until fork-tender, then crank the heat to crisp the edges.
  3. Warm the tortillasHeat each corn tortilla on a dry comal or skillet until soft and lightly blistered, about 30 seconds a side, and keep them wrapped in a clean towel so they steam. Double them up — two thin tortillas per taco is the street standard, and they won't tear under a wet filling.
  4. Build the tacoWarm tortilla, a pile of chopped protein, a spoon of salsa, a scatter of diced white onion and cilantro, a crumble of cotija or queso fresco, and a hard squeeze of lime. That's the whole architecture — resist the urge to bury it.
  5. Serve immediatelyA taco is an assembly, not a dish that waits. Serve them the second they're built, with extra salsa, lime wedges, and a bowl of beans on the side. Eat standing up if you're doing it right.
