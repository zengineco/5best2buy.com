Cacio e Pepe, and Where to Source It

Three ingredients — pasta, Pecorino, pepper — and absolutely nowhere to hide. Cacio e pepe is pure technique: get a glossy, emulsified sauce instead of a clumpy mess, and you've made one of the great Roman plates in twenty minutes. Here's exactly how, and where to buy the three things that are the whole dish.

The Ingredients — and where to get them
Pasta
Spaghetti or, traditionally, thick square-cut tonnarelli. Bronze-die pasta has a rough surface that grips the sauce and releases the starch that makes the emulsion work.

Pecorino Romano
The cheese is the sauce — sharp, salty aged sheep's-milk Pecorino, finely grated. A real aged cheese emulsifies; pre-shredded, anti-caked cheese seizes. (No Pecorino? A great aged parm is the honest stand-in.)

Part Two
The Method
  1. Toast the pepperCrack the peppercorns coarsely and toast them in a dry pan over medium heat for a minute, until they smell fragrant. Add a ladle of pasta water and let it steep — this makes a peppery base that flavors the whole dish.
  2. Cook the pastaBoil the pasta in lightly salted water, using less water than usual so it goes extra starchy. Pull it a minute shy of al dente and save several cups of the cloudy cooking water — it's your sauce.
  3. Make the cheese creamIn a bowl, stir the grated Pecorino with a little warm, not boiling pasta water into a smooth, lump-free paste. Water that's too hot scrambles the cheese into clumps — controlling that temperature is the entire secret.
  4. EmulsifyOff the heat, toss the drained pasta in the peppery pan, then add the Pecorino cream and more pasta water a splash at a time, tossing hard and constantly. It'll go from grainy to a glossy sauce that clings to every strand. Keep the pan off direct high heat.
  5. Serve nowPlate immediately, with a last grating of Pecorino and a crack of pepper. It tightens fast as it cools, so this is a dish you eat the moment it's done.
