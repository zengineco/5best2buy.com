Lasagna, and Where to Source It

Real lasagna alla Bolognese is a project, not a weeknight — a ragù simmered for hours, a béchamel scented with nutmeg, and layer after layer bound with Parmigiano. There are no shortcuts that don't show, which is exactly why the ingredients carry it: a proper crushed tomato, egg pasta with some tooth, cheese from an actual wheel. Here's the method, and where to buy the parts that make it worth the afternoon.

A Note on Origins
Where lasagna comes from — honestly
Layered baked-dough dishes are genuinely ancient — the Romans ate a version, and there are lasagna-like recipes in a 1300s manuscript — but the lasagna you know is younger than the legend suggests. The popular claim that it descends from Greek laganon is a plausible etymology, not proof: laganon was fried dough, not boiled or baked pasta. Two Italian regions have real claims — Naples for an early baked-with-cheese lineage, Emilia-Romagna / Bologna for the ragù-and-béchamel version the world knows — so we treat it as two traditions, not one true origin. And since tomatoes only reached Europe after 1492, any "ancient tomato lasagna" is impossible; the version here is at most a few centuries old. "The world's oldest pasta" is a nice line, but it's folklore, not fact.
Part Two
The Method
  1. Build the ragùSoften a fine soffritto of onion, carrot, and celery in olive oil, low and slow. Add the beef and pork and brown them properly. Stir in tomato paste, deglaze with the red wine, then add the crushed tomatoes, a splash of milk, and a bay leaf. Simmer as low as your stove goes for 2–3 hours — this is where the flavor is made.
  2. Make the béchamelMelt butter, whisk in an equal amount of flour and cook to a blond roux, then whisk in warm milk until smooth and thick enough to coat a spoon. Season with salt and a generous grating of fresh nutmeg — the note that says "Bolognese."
  3. Layer itIn a baking dish: a smear of ragù, then pasta, ragù, béchamel, a heavy snow of Parmigiano — and repeat for as many layers as you can build. (Swap in ricotta or torn mozzarella if you're going southern.) Finish with béchamel and a final blanket of Parmigiano.
  4. Bake375°F (190°C) for about 40 minutes, until it's bubbling at the edges and bronzed on top. If your sheets are no-boil or fresh, keep the sauces a touch loose so they cook through.
  5. RestLet it sit 15–20 minutes before you cut it — the layers need to set, or it slides into a delicious mess. The hardest step, and the one that separates a clean slice from a landslide.
