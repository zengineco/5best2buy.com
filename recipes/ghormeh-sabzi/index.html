Ghormeh Sabzi, and Where to Source It

The whole character of ghormeh sabzi comes from frying a mountain of herbs low and slow until they turn nearly black and smell nutty — rush this and the stew tastes grassy and flat. Dried Persian limes (limoo amani) give the deep sour funk that defines it; pierce them so they release inside. Fenugreek is small in volume but huge in flavor, so don't skip it.

The Method
  1. Fry the herbsChop all herbs fine and fry in oil over medium-low for 20-30 minutes until they darken and smell toasty, not grassy.
  2. Brown the baseIn a separate pot fry onion until golden, add turmeric, then brown the lamb cubes on all sides.
  3. Combine and simmerAdd the fried herbs, beans, pierced dried limes, water to cover, and simmer covered on low for 2.5-3 hours.
  4. Adjust the sourPress the limes against the side to release juice; if too bitter, remove one or two limes.
  5. ServeThe stew is done when the oil rises and the sauce is dark and thick; serve over basmati rice.

Straight Answers
Can you make Ghormeh Sabzi in an air fryer? — Yes, with an insert

Not on its own — an air fryer can’t hold a pool of liquid. You can simmer it in an oven-safe dish or a silicone insert made to fit the basket, but the broth won’t reduce or brown the way it does on the stove, and that insert comes out scorching, so use oven mitts. For real depth, a pot on the stove wins.

Can you freeze Ghormeh Sabzi? — Yes

Yes — it freezes well. Cool it fully, portion into airtight containers, and it keeps a few months; thaw in the fridge and reheat gently.

Can you make Ghormeh Sabzi ahead of time? — Yes

Yes — it holds well and often tastes better the next day. Make it ahead and reheat gently before serving.

Is Ghormeh Sabzi gluten-free? — Yes

Yes — nothing in this recipe as written contains gluten. As always, glance at the labels on any packaged items (broth, sauces, chocolate), since brands vary.

Is Ghormeh Sabzi vegan? — No

No — it contains meat, so it isn’t vegetarian or vegan.
