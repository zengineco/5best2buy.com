Wontons, and Where to Source Them

A wonton is "swallowing clouds" — a spoon of pork and shrimp folded into a skin so thin it ripples in the broth. It's simple food, which is exactly why the seasoning has to be right: a naturally-brewed soy sauce, a real toasted sesame oil, a whisper of white pepper. Get the filling and the folding down and you'll make bowls better than takeout, and freeze a hundred for later. Here's the method, and where to buy every part.

A Note on Origins
Where the wonton comes from — honestly
The wonton belongs to the vast northern-Chinese family of boiled dumplings, and its name carries a small piece of cosmology: Mandarin húntún (餛飩) echoes hùndùn (混沌), "chaos" or "primordial formlessness" — the old image of a sealed, shapeless dumpling. The Cantonese name, wan tan (雲吞), is lovelier still: literally "swallowing clouds," for the way they bob in the broth. What we'd rather not do is hand you a fake precision: you'll see confident claims that wontons were "invented in the Han Dynasty" or "perfected in the Tang," but those hard dates are folklore-adjacent, not documented. The safe truth is that wontons are ancient, that they diverged into regional forms over many centuries (the fiery Sichuan chāoshǒu, the Cantonese wonton-noodle bowl), and that a single "inventor" is a story, not a record.
Part Two
The Method
  1. Make the fillingMix the ground pork and chopped shrimp with soy sauce, toasted sesame oil, white pepper, Shaoxing wine, grated ginger, and scallion. Stir hard in one direction for a minute or two until the filling turns sticky and cohesive — that texture is what makes a springy, juicy wonton.
  2. Fold themA small spoon of filling in the center of each wrapper, wet the edges, fold into a triangle, then bring the two long corners together and pinch to seal. Don't overfill. Keep the wrappers and the finished wontons under a damp towel so they don't dry and crack.
  3. CookFor soup, drop them into boiling water and cook 3–4 minutes, until they float and the filling is cooked through, then lift into bowls of hot broth. For fried wontons, deep-fry until golden and crisp. Either way, don't crowd the pot.
  4. ServeWonton soup wants only a little greens, scallion, and a few drops of sesame oil; fried wontons want a dipping sauce. To stockpile, freeze the raw wontons on a tray until firm, then bag them — cook from frozen, straight into the pot.
